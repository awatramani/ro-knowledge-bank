Survey Logistics

Planning the Operation Before You Go

Managing a survey is a massive logistical undertaking. The decisions made in the weeks before fieldwork begins — about permissions, transport, equipment, and safety — determine whether the research runs smoothly or grinds to a halt on Day 1.

Research Associate asks:

"We have IRB clearance and a finalized instrument. Can we start fieldwork next week?"

Research Manager replies:

"IRB clearance is the starting gun, not the finish line. Vendor contracts, local authority permissions, transport logistics, and device procurement all take weeks. These must run in parallel with instrument development — not after it."

Permissions and Local Authorities

You may need permission to survey from district officials. In some countries, research permits are required from multiple levels of government. Plan for this as early as possible — in some areas you cannot begin survey activities before obtaining permission. Printed letters describing the survey activities, with contact information for local contacts and the PIs, are helpful. Failure to communicate survey plans with local authorities can delay your entire team.

Transport Contracting

Hiring vehicles involves searching for available hire companies, understanding costs and policies, and verifying that vendors meet all local legal requirements including licenses and tax certificates. Your signed contract should clearly define roles and responsibilities, what happens in case of breakdown, and who covers costs for fuel, parking, and driver meals — ambiguity on these points creates conflict in the field.

20% Buffer staff to hire above target team size
4–8 Surveys per surveyor per day — conservative estimate
10% Minimum back-check sample of completed interviews
🔑 Key Principle

Data collection is a logistics operation disguised as research. Without transport, permissions, power, and devices confirmed in advance, the research stops — regardless of how good the questionnaire is. Start logistics planning the day IRB approval is submitted, not the day it is received.

Safety Protocols

Safety considerations must be built into the logistics plan as standing protocols — not judgment calls made in the moment. Field teams should not survey during periods of political instability, after dark, or in weather that creates dangerous road conditions. Field staff must be explicitly told they are authorized to stop work and abandon a target if they feel unsafe. Data is never worth a safety risk.

Logistics Red Flags
  • Starting vendor search after instrument is locked.
  • No written contract with transport providers.
  • Assuming driver costs are self-covered.
  • No contingency cash for field emergencies.
What Good Looks Like
  • Logistics planning begins when IRB is submitted.
  • Contracts signed before training begins.
  • Per diem terms communicated clearly before Day 1.
  • Contingency petty cash allocated per team.

Surveyor Hiring & Training

Building a Field Team That Delivers

Finding resilient staff is more important than finding experienced staff. Fieldwork requires grit, patience, and the ability to listen — qualities that a resume cannot reveal and a mock interview will.

Working Out How Many to Hire

Work backwards from the target field team size. Define the end target using the sample size together with conservative assumptions of how many surveys can be completed per day — accounting for transport, unanticipated challenges, and respondent unavailability. If you anticipate a high dropout rate, invite an additional 20% of prospective surveyors to training so that training itself becomes the final selection process.

What to Look For

For candidates with no prior experience, in-depth screening is essential. Key things to assess: language fluency in the survey language, ability to hold a conversation while entering data on a device, basic software skills, and composure under pressure. Do not just ask survey questions during screening — roleplay a hostile or distressed respondent scenario and see who stays calm, who listens, and who tries to argue or rush.

The Hiring Scenario That Tells You Everything Candidate A has five years of experience but interrupts you during the mock interview and argues about question wording. Candidate B is a fresher who listens carefully, asks clarifying questions, and handles a simulated hostile respondent with patience. Candidate A's experience is a liability. Candidate B is your hire.

What Training Must Cover

A good training agenda covers: project objectives and why the study exists, the code of conduct and termination offenses, informed consent protocols, research ethics, a full CAPI walkthrough on the actual tablet models, and at least one full day of field practice with real respondents in conditions similar to the actual study area. Identify top performers during practice — not just on paper — and consider gender composition and language diversity when building the final team.

⚠️ Screening Red Flags
  • Cannot explain the study purpose in plain language after training.
  • Shows impatience or interrupts during mock respondent exercises.
  • Insufficient language fluency in the survey language.
  • Rigid or defensive when a protocol is questioned.
✅ Signs of a Strong Candidate
  • Listens fully before responding — even when flustered.
  • Asks clarifying questions rather than guessing.
  • Calm and respectful under a hostile mock respondent.
  • Reports a problem rather than quietly working around it.

Field Team Management

Structure, Communication, and Daily Operations

Depending on team size, you may need to manage upwards of 50 field staff. Thinking ahead about reporting structures, payment processes, and safety protocols ensures the survey runs without delays.

Reporting Structure

Surveyors should be divided into smaller teams and report directly to a supervisor. Supervisors report to a field manager, project associate, or research associate depending on team structure. A ratio of one supervisor to four or six surveyors is recommended for active oversight. Clear lines of escalation must be established before fieldwork begins — every surveyor must know who to call and when.

Daily Debriefs

Daily debrief sessions with the entire team are recommended during the initial weeks of field launch. These sessions surface problems early, resolve confusion about protocols, track daily progress against targets, and maintain team morale during difficult stretches. They are not optional — they are the primary quality control mechanism for the first two weeks.

🔑 Management Principle

The field manager who is in daily contact with their team catches problems in hours. The one who checks in weekly catches them after the damage is done — after surveys have been conducted incorrectly, data has been compromised, and correction is no longer possible.

Payments and Field Allowances

Per diem rates, travel allowances, and reimbursement processes must be communicated clearly before Day 1. Ambiguity on financial terms is one of the most common sources of field team conflict. If surveyors use personal motorcycles, reimburse by distance traveled rather than allowing self-managed invoices — this reduces both fraud risk and disputes.

Poor Management Signs
  • No daily debrief — problems accumulate silently.
  • Supervisor-to-surveyor ratio too high to monitor.
  • Payment terms unclear — creates conflict on Day 2.
  • Safety protocols never explicitly communicated.
Good Management Signs
  • Issues resolved within 24 hours via daily debrief.
  • One supervisor per 4–6 surveyors for active oversight.
  • Per diem and allowance terms set before fieldwork begins.
  • Staff explicitly authorized to stop work if unsafe.

Questionnaire Piloting

Testing, Refining, and Testing Again

Piloting is the testing, refining, and re-testing of survey instruments in the field to make them ready for full deployment. A successful pilot is one that finds errors — if you come back saying it worked perfectly, you did not test it hard enough.

The Three Phases of Piloting

Brand new survey instruments go through three phases. First, cognitive testing of individual questions to check comprehension and phrasing. Second, a small-scale field pilot to test the full instrument flow and skip patterns in realistic conditions. Third, a final pre-launch pilot on the actual tablet models, in the actual study area, with respondents representative of the actual sample. Each phase catches different classes of errors.

Tracking What the Pilot Reveals

Each enumerator should carry a form for recording piloting metadata — survey timing, respondent type, and high-level observations about question clarity and flow. This is not optional record-keeping. It is the mechanism by which the research team systematically identifies what to fix between pilot rounds. Without it, the same errors surface in the full launch.

The False Positive Pilot You piloted in a quiet classroom with cooperative colleagues. It went perfectly. Day 1 in the field: the village is noisy, onlookers crowd the surveyor, and the respondent gets distracted every two minutes. Your pilot validated nothing because it tested nothing real. Pilot in the hardest conditions you can find — not the easiest.

✅ Pilot Well

  • Pilot in conditions matching the real study area.
  • Record metadata for every pilot interview.
  • Iterate: test, fix, test again before locking.
  • Pilot field protocols, not just the questionnaire.

❌ Common Mistakes

  • One pilot round only — treating a clean run as sign-off.
  • Using familiar, cooperative respondents.
  • Testing on different tablet models than the field ones.
  • Skipping logistics during the pilot — only testing the form.

Third-Party Survey Firms

When to Outsource and How to Stay in Control

Conducting a survey is a significant logistical undertaking. One of the first decisions research teams face is whether to outsource data collection or run the survey in-house — and the answer depends on capacity, context, and what you are willing to trade off.

When Outsourcing Makes Sense

Research teams may decide to contract a survey firm if the organization has limited capacity or experience working with a given population, and there are established firms in the study area with relevant experience. However, survey firms may not have experience working on RCTs, may be more expensive, and contract negotiations can be complex and time-consuming.

Budget Scrutiny

When reviewing a firm's budget, assess whether it reasonably reflects all research activities — including surveyor salaries and pay for training days. Compare against similar projects where possible. Key items that can sometimes be negotiated include questionnaire design, programming, and translation — especially if your team has internal capacity for these. Push back on productivity assumptions: a firm estimating 12 surveys per surveyor per day in a rural context is not being realistic.

🔑 The Non-Negotiable

Some firms may object to requiring their surveyors to sign confidentiality agreements. On any J-PAL project, this is non-negotiable. If a firm resists, understand their data protection processes in detail and push back if necessary. PII must be encrypted. Data transfer protocols must meet your IRB requirements. These are not preferences — they are conditions.

Maintaining Research Authority

Even when outsourcing, the research team retains full responsibility for designing, reviewing, and approving the study and the survey. The firm executes; the research team owns. Establish clear protocols for what the firm can and cannot change without approval, how data is transferred and stored, and what quality check procedures the firm is required to follow.

⚠️ Watch For These
  • Firm lacks RCT experience — may not understand why control groups matter.
  • Unrealistic daily productivity assumptions in the budget.
  • Resistance to confidentiality agreement requirements.
  • Vague data transfer and encryption protocols.
✅ Signs of a Good Firm
  • Prior experience with RCTs or J-PAL/IPA projects.
  • Realistic productivity estimates backed by prior project data.
  • Clear confidentiality and PII encryption protocols.
  • Open to back-check and spot-check requirements.

Logistics Readiness

Every dependency must be confirmed before the first team departs.

Local authority permissions obtained. Letters prepared with PI contact information.

Vehicle contracts finalized. Vendor meets legal requirements. Roles and responsibilities clearly defined.
✓
Base camp confirmed with reliable power for overnight tablet charging.
✓
Contingency petty cash allocated. Per diem terms communicated to all field staff.
✓
Safety protocols established: no surveying during instability, after dark, or in dangerous weather.

⚠️ The Trap

Starting logistics planning after IRB clearance. Permissions, contracts, and equipment procurement each take weeks. They must run in parallel with instrument development — not after it.

Team Readiness

Every member of the field team must meet these standards before deployment.

✓
All surveyors completed training including at least one day of field practice with real respondents.
✓
20% buffer staff recruited and trained to absorb field attrition.
✓
Reporting structure confirmed — every surveyor knows their supervisor and escalation path.
✓
Daily debrief schedule established for the first two weeks of fieldwork.
✓
If using a survey firm: confidentiality agreements signed, data transfer protocols confirmed.

Instrument Readiness

The questionnaire must be locked, translated, and fully piloted before the first interview.

✓
All translations reviewed. Back-translation completed with acceptable discrepancy rate.
✓
Questionnaire piloted in realistic field conditions — not a classroom. Errors found and corrected.
✓
Logical flows and skip patterns tested on the actual tablet models going to the field.
✓
Instrument version locked. All surveyors have the correct final version on their device.

⚠️ The Trap

Piloting once and treating a clean run as sign-off. Piloting is iterative — test, refine, test again. A pilot that finds no problems almost certainly did not test hard enough.

Quality Systems

Data quality systems must be active from Day 1 — not set up after problems appear.

✓
HFC protocol established — who runs checks, when, and what thresholds trigger escalation.
✓
Back-check sample defined — minimum 10% of completed interviews re-contacted independently.
✓
Spot-check schedule in place — supervisor observation plan for the first two weeks.
✓
All surveyors briefed that quality checks are standard procedure — not a signal of distrust.
🔑 Closing Principle

High-frequency checks, back-checks, and spot-checks can detect programming errors, surveyor errors, data fabrication, and poorly understood questions. Start them on Day 1 — not when something looks wrong.
