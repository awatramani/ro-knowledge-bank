1.1 · Who We Are

J-PAL South Asia & the Research Operations Division

J-PAL South Asia (J-PAL SA) is a regional office of the Abdul Latif Jameel Poverty Action Lab, based at IFMR at Krea University. We conduct rigorous randomised evaluations of social programmes across South Asia — in health, agriculture, education, livelihoods, financial services, and social protection. Our findings inform policy decisions that affect millions of people.

The Research Operations (ResOps) Division is the team responsible for making that research happen on the ground. We build and manage the field infrastructure — the people, the systems, the quality controls, and the ethical frameworks — that turn a research design into real data. Every surveyor who enters a household, every tablet that uploads a completed form, every consent form that goes into a locked cabinet: that is ResOps work.

🎯

Why This Matters for You

You are not just a data collector. You are a representative of an institution whose credibility depends on the integrity of every field interaction. A fabricated survey, a lost consent form, or a harassment complaint from a surveyor is not just an individual mistake — it is an organisational incident. That weight is not meant to intimidate you; it is meant to help you understand why the standards exist.

1.2 · Role Mandates

Both RAs and PAs are critical to field operations, but their responsibilities differ in scope, accountability, and decision-making authority. Understanding where your role ends and someone else's begins is not bureaucracy — it is what makes teams work.

⚠️ The Delegation Trap — Critical for RAs

Delegation is not abdication. You can delegate execution to PAs and SCs, but you cannot delegate accountability. When the FFR finds a deviation, the question is not "Who was supposed to do this?" — it is "RA, why didn't your system catch this?" Build the system. Don't just do the tasks.

ℹ️ For PAs — You Are the Field Backbone

PAs make or break field operations. An RA who has a highly capable PA can focus on quality and strategy. An RA with a PA who filters information, avoids difficult tasks, or cuts corners on PII will be constantly firefighting. Your reliability and transparency are your most important professional assets.

🗣️ Field Scenario — Bhopal, Madhya Pradesh

The Situation: RA Priyanka was managing a nutrition survey across 3 districts in MP. She delegated all field coordination to her PA, Rahul, and focused on data analysis. In Week 4, Priyanka discovered through a back-check that 38 households listed as "complete" were actually visited by the supervisor — not the surveyor — and the consent forms showed only 1 signature template, clearly copied. Rahul had known but hadn't escalated because "Priyanka seemed very busy."

The project had to re-survey 38 households (3 days, ₹22,000), re-visit all consent forms in that block, and file an IRB incident report. Priyanka's quarterly review cited "inadequate field oversight" despite her excellent data analysis.

📌 Key Learning: An RA's job is to build direct field connect — not rely solely on the PA as the information conduit. Parallel communication lines are not optional; they are how you catch what doesn't get escalated.

RAs must maintain direct contact with at least 3-4 surveyors per team — not to bypass the PA, but to have an independent read on field conditions. This is called a parallel communication line. 🚩 The Silence Problem

The single most dangerous dynamic in field teams is one where junior staff feel they cannot share bad news with senior staff. If surveyors are afraid to tell supervisors about non-contacts, if supervisors are afraid to tell PAs about data quality issues, and if PAs are afraid to tell RAs about problems — by the time the RA finds out, it will have been going on for weeks. Build a culture where reporting problems is rewarded, not punished.

Safety & Security in the Field

Core Policy · Safety & Security

Your Safety Is Non-Negotiable

No survey, target, or deadline is worth a safety compromise. J-PAL South Asia requires all field staff to follow safety protocols — not because something always goes wrong, but because the consequences when something does go wrong without a protocol are severe and irreversible. These rules are operational requirements, not suggestions.

  • Never work alone in remote or unfamiliar areas. Field staff should always operate in pairs or teams, especially in new villages, urban informal settlements, or areas with reported safety concerns.
  • Set a daily check-in time. Every team must confirm safe return to base by a defined time (typically by 7:00 PM). If no check-in by cut-off time, the supervisor must immediately contact the PA, and the PA must contact the RA.
  • Do not work after dark. Field operations must conclude at least 30 minutes before sunset unless explicitly approved for specific evening surveys. Evening surveys require written protocol, a designated male and female escort, and a confirmed safe return system.
  • Know the emergency contact protocol. Every team member must have the PA's number, the supervisor's number, and the project emergency number saved. The project must maintain a "field emergency card" with local police, hospital, and J-PAL contact details.
  • Assess and adapt. If a community feels unsafe on a given day — local conflict, hostile group, hostile community leader — teams have the authority to withdraw and report to the PA/RA without completing their target. No surveyor should be pressured to enter an unsafe situation to meet numbers.

🗣️ Field Scenario — Araria, Bihar

The Situation: A surveyor team of two women in Araria district had completed their daily assignments and were returning to base on a motorcycle at 7:30 PM. They had not checked in at the 7 PM cut-off. The supervisor tried calling — no answer (network area). PA Neha escalated to RA Ajay at 7:45 PM. By 8:15 PM, with local contacts mobilised and the supervisor dispatched to the last known route, the team was found safe — the motorcycle had a puncture and one phone had run out of battery.

No one was hurt. But the protocol that triggered the search — the missed 7 PM check-in — was the reason anyone went looking. Without that protocol, the team might have waited alone on an unlit road for hours with no one knowing.

📌 Key Learning: Safety protocols are designed for the one time they're needed. The 99 times nothing goes wrong, the check-in feels routine. The one time it doesn't — it is the difference between a minor incident and a crisis.
  • Project emergency card printed and distributed to all team members
  • Daily check-in time set and communicated to all supervisors and PAs
  • Team pairing rule communicated — no solo field visits in new areas
  • Evening cut-off time established (default: 30 min before sunset)
  • Local emergency contact numbers (police, hospital) saved in project phone
  • Escalation protocol: Supervisor → PA → RA → RM within defined timeframes

2.1 · Team Composition

Building the Right Team for Your Survey

Your first operational decision is team architecture — how many people, in what roles, covering what geography. Get this wrong and you will either overspend or under-deliver. The right team structure flows from your sample design, not from what was convenient on a previous project.

✅ The Team Sizing Formula

Step 1: Define your daily target — how many completed surveys per day across the project?
Step 2: Estimate per-surveyor daily productivity: 3–5 surveys/day (HH, rural), 5–7/day (urban SAS), 10–15/day (CATI).
Step 3: Divide target by per-surveyor productivity. Add 15% buffer for refusals, travel, re-visits.
Step 4: Organise surveyors into teams of 4–6 per supervisor.
Step 5: Add 1 monitor per 2–3 teams.

Example: Target = 40 surveys/day. At 4/surveyor/day → 10 surveyors + 15% = 12. 2 teams of 6, each with 1 supervisor. 1 monitor. Total: 12 + 2 + 1 = 15 field staff.

🗣️ Field Scenario — Hoshiarpur, Punjab

The Situation: RA Deepika designed her team for a rural health survey with 1 supervisor per 10 surveyors — following a previous project template from an urban SAS survey. In the field, supervisors were unable to do daily accompaniments, couldn't collect consent forms each evening from all 10 surveyors, and missed data quality issues for days at a time. By Week 3, the back-check failure rate was 18% (acceptable is under 5%).

The problem was not the surveyors — it was that 1 supervisor for 10 surveyors is a rural HH ratio that simply doesn't work. Urban SAS allows higher ratios because teams are geographically concentrated. Rural surveys require supervisors to travel between villages and need lower ratios.

📌 Key Learning: Team ratios are context-dependent. Never copy team structures from a different survey context. Build from the productivity and geography realities of your specific project.

The Two-File PII System — Critical

The FFR specifically asks: "Do the trackers contain respondent PII such as Name, Phone, Address, or ID details?" Most teams inadvertently embed PII in operational trackers. The solution is the Two-File System.

✅ File A — Operational Tracker (Shared)

Contains: Respondent codes, village codes, survey status, surveyor code, date, flags. No names, no phones, no addresses. Visible to all field staff. Shareable in team WhatsApp for daily updates.

ℹ️ File B — PII Linkage File (Restricted)

Maps codes to names, phones, and addresses. Access restricted to RA and SC only — view-only, no download. Stored in a protected Google Sheet. PAs access only with RA authorisation for specific tasks.

🚩 The Silent PII Violation

Many RAs don't realise their tracking sheets contain PII. If your performance tracker includes respondent names or phone numbers alongside survey IDs — that is a PII document and must be treated as one. Check every tracker before deployment: if you can identify a specific household member from it, it contains PII.

🗣️ Field Scenario — Patna, Bihar

The Situation: PA Suresh was coordinating respondent visits via the team WhatsApp group. He shared a screenshot of the tracking sheet that included respondent names, phone numbers, and pregnancy status alongside village names. A surveyor's phone was stolen at a bus stand in Danapur. The respondent data — including sensitive health information — was now on an unsecured, stolen device. The project had to notify the IRB, contact all affected respondents, and implement emergency data protection measures.

📌 Key Learning: WhatsApp is a coordination tool, not a data management platform. Never share tracking sheets, respondent lists, or any PII through WhatsApp. Design your trackers so that the version shared in WhatsApp contains zero PII — only codes and status flags.

3.1 · Research Ethics

Why Ethics Is a Field Issue — Not a Classroom Concept

Research ethics is not a box to tick during training — it is the operating system of everything you do in the field. Every decision you make about how to approach a respondent, how to administer consent, how to handle a disclosure of harm, and how to protect the data you collect is an ethical decision. This session makes those abstract principles concrete and field-specific.

The Four Core Principles — Applied to Field Work

PrincipleWhat It MeansHow It Plays Out in the Field
Respect for PersonsRespondents are autonomous individuals with the right to decide whether to participateInformed consent is not a formality. The respondent must genuinely understand what they are agreeing to. Rushing consent violates this principle — even if the form is signed.
BeneficenceThe research should benefit participants and society; at minimum, it should not harm themIf a respondent discloses domestic violence, mental illness, or child abuse during your survey, you have a duty of care that extends beyond the questionnaire. Know the referral protocols.
Non-MaleficenceDo not cause harm through the research process itselfAsking sensitive questions about income, violence, or sexual health can be harmful in certain contexts — especially in the presence of a spouse or authority figure. Ensure privacy before sensitive modules.
JusticeResearch benefits and burdens should be fairly distributedDo not systematically exclude certain households (e.g., distant ones, difficult interviewers) to make your job easier. The sample is designed to be representative — substituting undermines justice.

🔬 IRB Approval Is Not a Blanket Clearance

The IRB approves your research protocol — the way you described you would conduct the study. Every deviation from that protocol (using a modified consent form, skipping a module for "difficult" respondents, changing the respondent eligibility criteria in the field) is a protocol violation that may require IRB notification. When in doubt about a field adaptation, consult your RA/RM before implementing it.

3.2 · Informed Consent in Practice

Consent Is a Process — Not a Signature

The most important sentence in research ethics training: consent is a process, not a signature on a form. The respondent must genuinely understand — in their own language, at their own pace — who you are, what the research is about, what you will ask, how long it will take, that their answers are confidential, and that they can stop at any time. A surveyor who rushes through the consent script in 30 seconds and hands a pen to the respondent has violated the consent process — even if the form is signed.

The Six Elements of Valid Informed Consent

#ElementWhat to Say (Example)What Makes It Invalid
1Who you are"Mera naam Kavita hai. Main IFMR ke liye kaam karti hoon…"Introducing yourself only by first name with no organisational affiliation
2Purpose of the research"Hum ek study kar rahe hain jo ki bachon ki sehat ke baare mein hai…"Vague descriptions like "just a survey" or "the government sent us"
3What you will ask"Main aapse aapke ghar ke kharche, bacchon ki padhai, aur sehat ke baare mein puchhunga…"Not telling respondents the topics of questions in advance
4Duration"Isme lagbhag 35-40 minute lagenge"Understating the time to avoid refusals
5Confidentiality"Aapke jawaab sirf research team ke log dekhenge. Aapka naam kabhi bhi report mein nahi aayega."Vague statements or omitting this element entirely
6Right to stop"Aap kabhi bhi iss survey se bahar nikal sakte hain. Kisi bhi sawaal ka jawab dena zaroori nahi hai."Not explicitly stating the right to withdraw; rushing past it

🚩 Consent Invalidators — Know These

  • Coercion: "The sarpanch said everyone must participate" — this is coercion. Consent must be voluntary.
  • Deception: "We're from the government" or "You'll get a certificate/benefit" (unless true and approved by IRB)
  • Rushing: Reading the consent script while the respondent is clearly distracted or has not been given time to process
  • Child signing: A child under 18 cannot provide consent. A parent or guardian must sign for them.
  • Proxy consent: "Her husband said it's fine" — the husband's permission is not the woman's consent. Each eligible respondent must consent for themselves.
  • Incentive pressure: "If you complete this survey you get ₹50" — incentives are permitted if IRB-approved, but they must not be the reason someone feels they cannot refuse.
🗣️ Field Scenario — Varanasi, Uttar Pradesh

The Situation: Surveyor Pankaj was running 2 hours behind schedule. At household #12, an elderly woman named Savitri opened the door. Pankaj handed her the consent form and said "Sign here please, madam — it's for a study about health." Savitri, who could not read, picked up the pen and made her thumbprint without understanding anything about the study. Pankaj completed the survey in 22 minutes (designed for 35) and moved on.

In a routine back-check, the back-checker asked Savitri what the study was about. She said: "I don't know — a young man came and asked some questions." She had no recollection of being told about confidentiality or her right to stop.

The consent was deemed invalid. The survey had to be re-administered with a proper consent process — adding 2 field days and flagging Pankaj for training remediation.

📌 Key Learning: Thumbprints without understanding are not consent. When a respondent cannot read, the surveyor must read the entire consent form aloud in the respondent's language before the thumbprint is obtained. "Signed" and "consented" are not the same thing.

Core Policy · Mandatory

POSH — Prevention of Sexual Harassment

Field deployments concentrate the exact conditions where POSH risks are elevated: power differentials between supervisors and surveyors, physical isolation from institutional support, long working hours in unfamiliar settings, and a culture of normalising difficult conditions as "that's just how fieldwork is." This section exists because these risks are real — and everyone on a field team has rights and responsibilities under the POSH framework.

  • You can report to your PA, the RA, the SC, the RO Team Lead, or directly to the Internal Complaints Committee (ICC). You do not need to first tell the person accused.
  • Reports can be made in writing, verbally, or through a WhatsApp message to any of the above — whatever you feel safe using.
  • You will not be penalised for making a complaint in good faith. Retaliation against a complainant is itself a violation.
  • All complaints are handled confidentially. The names of the complainant and accused are not shared outside the ICC process.

For RAs and PAs — Your Responsibilities

  • You cannot dismiss, trivialise, or "resolve informally" a POSH complaint. Once raised, it must be escalated.
  • If a team member discloses harassment to you, your first action is to ensure their immediate safety — reassign them away from the alleged harasser if needed, same day.
  • Do not investigate the complaint yourself. Acknowledge, document, and escalate to the ICC/RO Team Lead within 24 hours.
  • Create team cultures where POSH training is taken seriously — not treated as a compliance box. The tone set by senior team members determines whether junior staff feel safe reporting.
📌 Field Nuance: Female surveyors working alone in communities face POSH risks from community members, not just colleagues. J-PAL SA's broader Dignity at Work policy covers these situations. If a community member's behaviour makes a surveyor feel unsafe or harassed, this should be escalated to the PA/RA — the same day — and the surveyor should not be sent alone to that area until the situation is assessed.

Core Policy · Mandatory

Child Protection in the Field

Field deployments bring research staff into communities where children live, play, work, and sometimes suffer harm. In household surveys, children appear as household members, as bystanders during interviews, and occasionally as the subject of specific modules. Every interaction with a child — intentional or incidental — carries responsibility, because children cannot fully protect their own interests in the presence of adult strangers with institutional authority.

Core Principles — Do No Harm

PrincipleWhat It Requires in the Field
Never interview a child without specific IRB approval and parental consentChildren under 18 are not eligible respondents unless the study protocol specifically includes them with IRB-approved methods. Even then, a parent or guardian must consent and ideally be present.
Never be alone with a childNo one-on-one interactions with a child who is not your own family member. This protects the child and protects you from any accusation of inappropriate conduct.
Never photograph a child without explicit parental consentEven incidental photographs — site photos, team photos — that include identifiable children require parental consent. Ask before you photograph.
Protect children's data with maximum confidentialityNames, ages, school enrollment, health status, or any other information about children collected in the survey is never shared outside the project team.
Social media: absolute zero tolerance for child imagesNo image of a child may be posted to any personal social media account — Instagram, WhatsApp status, Facebook, X — regardless of context. This is a termination-level violation.

When a Child Discloses Harm — Your 4 Steps

  • Step 1 — Stop probing. Do not ask follow-up questions about the harm. You are not a trained forensic interviewer. Additional questioning can contaminate future legal processes and re-traumatise the child.
  • Step 2 — Listen without reacting dramatically. If the child or parent is speaking, listen. Do not minimise ("I'm sure it's not that bad") or dramatise ("We need to call the police right now"). A calm, neutral presence helps the child feel safe.
  • Step 3 — Record verbatim. Write down exactly what was said or observed in your field diary. Time-stamped, word-for-word. Do not interpret or summarise at this stage.
  • Step 4 — Escalate same day. Report to your PA or RA the same day. Do not decide alone whether it is "serious enough." The PA/RA decides in consultation with the RO Team Lead and the project's safeguarding focal point. The escalation decision is theirs — not the surveyor's.
📌 Important: If you observe a child in immediate physical danger, you may alert a nearby adult authority (police, teacher, health worker) in addition to your escalation chain — but do not take direct personal action beyond that. Ensure your own safety and the safety of your team first.
🗣️ Field Scenario — Jabalpur, Madhya Pradesh

The Situation: During a household survey on education, surveyor Mamta arrived at a home where a 9-year-old girl answered the door. The girl said her parents were at work. The mother was the eligible respondent for the survey. Mamta thought: "Let me just ask the girl a few questions to fill the household roster while I wait — it won't take long."

What Mamta should have done: Wait outside or in a public space, not enter the home alone with the child. If the parents cannot be reached within a reasonable time, mark the household as "Not at home — revisit" and leave. Never conduct an informal interview with a child, even to fill in household roster data.

📌 Key Learning: "Just a few questions" is not a justification for unsupervised access to a child. An inconvenient re-visit is a small price to pay for child safety compliance.

4.1 · SurveyCTO

SurveyCTO Navigation — Step by Step

Every surveyor must be able to open, complete, and sync a form confidently before their first field day. This walkthrough covers the core steps. Facilitator note: this section requires tablets in hand. Run through each step with the group doing it live.

1

Open SurveyCTO Collect

Tap the SurveyCTO Collect icon on the tablet home screen. You will see "Fill Blank Form" and "Edit Saved Form" options.

⚠️ Common error: Opening Google Chrome instead of SurveyCTO Collect. The icons look different — Collect has a clipboard logo.

2

Select the Correct Form Version

Tap "Fill Blank Form." A list of downloaded forms appears. Always check the form version date — if multiple versions are listed, use the most recent one.

⚠️ Common error: Selecting an outdated form version. Old versions may not contain updated skip patterns or question wording. If unsure, check with your supervisor before starting.

3

Read the Consent Screen — In Full

The form opens with the consent screen as the first page. Read this aloud to the respondent in their language before tapping "Next." Do not skip or rush the consent screen.

⚠️ Common error: Tapping "Next" on the consent screen before obtaining the respondent's agreement. This is a data integrity violation and an IRB breach.

4

GPS Capture — Wait for the Lock

When the form requests GPS, hold the tablet still and wait until the accuracy reaches ≤15 metres. If you are indoors, step outside for a moment. Never manually enter GPS coordinates.

⚠️ Common error: Tapping "Accept" on a GPS reading with 80+ metre accuracy. This makes the GPS verification layer in the QC system flag the form.

5

Hard Stops vs. Soft Flags

Hard stops (red): The form will not proceed until the error is corrected. Re-read the question and check your entry. Common causes: entering text in a numeric field, selecting an ineligible combination of answers.

Soft flags (yellow): The form allows you to proceed, but the answer you entered is outside the expected range. Check with the respondent before continuing — do not simply ignore the flag.

6

Save and Sync — Before Leaving the Field

At end of the field day, go to "Send Finalized Form." Connect to WiFi or mobile data. Tap "Send Selected" or "Send All." Wait for the confirmation tick before leaving the network area.

⚠️ Common error: Returning to base without syncing. Forms stuck on "Finalized" overnight are a data quality risk — if the device is damaged or lost, those surveys are lost. Sync every day before you leave the field.

7

What to Do When Something Goes Wrong

Form crashes: Force close and re-open. The form auto-saves; you will usually continue where you left off. Battery dies mid-form: Charge and re-open. Do not start a new form — find the saved draft. Wrong respondent: Do not submit — mark as "Test/Error" in the survey ID field and flag to supervisor for deletion.

✅ The End-of-Day Sync Rule

No surveyor leaves the field with unsynchronised finalized forms. The supervisor must visually confirm sync completion (all forms show a green check) before allowing team departure. This is not optional — it is a daily accountability check that prevents data loss.

4.3 · Detection Architecture

The 5-Layer Fabrication Detection System

Every surveyor who has been caught fabricating believed they would not be caught. The detection architecture makes that belief false. These five layers operate simultaneously — data fabrication leaves traces across all of them.

L1

Duration Risk Review — Timestamp Analysis

SurveyCTO records the exact time from the first question to SUBMIT. A 35-minute survey completed in 8 minutes is statistically impossible to explain as a "fast respondent" — especially when the same surveyor has 6 such forms in a day.

Detection reliability: Very High
L2

GPS Verification — Location Cross-Check

GPS coordinates captured in the form are cross-checked against the sample frame. Interviews claimed in Village X but GPS showing the surveyor was in town, at home, or at the field office are flagged immediately.

Detection reliability: High
L3

Back-Check — Re-Interview Verification

A monitor calls or re-visits 5–10% of completed surveys and asks the respondent a set of back-check questions. Discrepancies between the original survey and the back-check trigger a deeper investigation.

Detection reliability: Very High (when done correctly)
L4

Statistical Outlier Detection — Pattern Analysis

Automated analysis flags unusual patterns: identical response distributions across forms, implausible answer combinations, suspiciously low variance in numeric responses, or clusters of answers that don't match geographic or demographic expectations.

Detection reliability: Medium-High (catches systematic fabrication)
L5

Accompaniment & Spot Check — Live Observation

Supervisors and monitors conduct unannounced accompaniments (observing the full interview process) and spot checks (brief visits to confirm a household was actually visited and the respondent exists).

Detection reliability: Definitive for surveyors being checked
🗣️ Field Scenario — Remote CATI Survey, Hyderabad

The Situation: CATI Surveyor Shyam's daily completion rate was 18 surveys/day when the team average was 11. Duration risk review showed his last 6 surveys each day averaged 4 minutes — against a designed 18-minute survey. GPS showed his phone stationary at the same location for all calls.

On Day 9, the supervisor conducted a silent monitoring session. Shyam dialled a number, waited 4 minutes without connecting, and entered fabricated responses. He was removed from the project the same day. 47 of his surveys were invalidated and had to be recollected — adding 5 additional field days.

📌 Key Learning: A suspiciously high completion rate is a red flag, not a performance achievement. Duration + GPS + back-check together form an almost-inescapable detection net. The cost of fabrication to the fabricator is termination; the cost to the project is time, money, and data quality. Neither is worth it.
4.4 · QC Architecture

Quality Control Infrastructure — Three Core Tools

1. Accompaniments

An accompaniment is when a supervisor or monitor observes the full survey process — from household approach through consent, interview, and form submission. This is the most powerful QC tool because it catches problems in real time.

ElementStandard
FrequencyMinimum 1 accompaniment per surveyor per week during active collection; daily during the first 3 days for new surveyors
Who conductsSupervisor for surveyors; PA/SC for supervisors; RA for PAs
What to observeConsent process (did they read the full script?), question reading accuracy (verbatim or paraphrased?), privacy (is the interview private?), response entry (entering what respondent says or interpreting?)
Feedback timingImmediate — after the interview, not at end of day. Delayed feedback is processed feedback and loses specificity.
FFR check"How many accompaniments have been conducted? What was documented?"

2. Spot Checks

A spot check is an unannounced brief visit to confirm that a survey actually happened — that the household was visited, the respondent exists, and basic facts match the form.

ElementStandard
SelectionRandom — not targeted at suspected fabricators (to maintain detection surprise)
Sample rate5–10% of completed surveys, minimum 3 per surveyor per week
Questions to askDid someone from [org] visit? What did they ask about? How long did it take? Did you sign something?
What triggers a deeper investigationRespondent unaware of visit; respondent description of survey duration dramatically different from recorded time; respondent denies specific modules that were recorded as completed

3. Back-Checks

A back-check re-administers a subset of questions from the original survey to the same respondent. It is designed to detect both fabrication (invented responses) and enumerator error (systematic misrecording).

ElementStandard
Selection5–10% of all completed surveys, randomly selected. Include at least 2 surveys per surveyor per week.
Question selectionChoose "stable" questions — those whose true answers don't change between interviews (e.g., household composition, asset ownership, respondent age). Avoid questions where recall naturally varies.
Acceptable discrepancy rateUnder 5% — if a surveyor's back-check discrepancy rate exceeds 10%, escalate to RA for investigation
DocumentationAll back-checks entered in back-check tracker with outcome: Pass / Minor Discrepancy / Major Discrepancy / Fabrication Suspected

✅ The 24-Hour Rule — The Single Most Important PII Habit

Hardcopy PII documents should not remain with field staff for more than 24 hours. This is the operational standard that prevents the accumulation of unsecured PII in the field. Supervisor collects at evening debrief — not the next morning. If the team is in a remote area without a field base, the supervisor keeps all documents in a locked bag and transfers them to the field office within 48 hours maximum.

🚩 The Ghost Access Problem

In several projects, access to tracking sheets with respondent PII remained active for months after fieldwork ended — because no one ran a deliberate access revocation process. Former surveyors could still open Google Sheets containing respondent names, phone numbers, and survey responses. This is a serious IRB concern. Data access revocation is a checklist task, done on Day 1 post-fieldwork — not a memory exercise.

5.4 · Project Wrap-Up

Closing a Project Correctly — The Final Checklist

Project wrap-up is the most under-managed phase of field operations. When fieldwork ends, urgency fades — and important closure tasks get delayed or forgotten. Build the wrap-up checklist into your project plan from Day 1 so it has the same status as everything else.

  • All surveys synced and confirmed on SurveyCTO server — zero pending submissions verified by SC
  • Google Sheet access revoked for all field staff — only RA, SC, and RO retain access
  • SurveyCTO/ODK server password changed; all surveyor login credentials deactivated
  • All WhatsApp group chats and media cleared; field staff demoted from admin roles
  • All device data cleared: tablets factory reset; project phones wiped; personal phone supervised deletion session completed
  • Asset return scheduled: all field staff return all devices and accessories on a single day
  • Asset register updated: IMEI verified at return, condition documented, damage deductions processed per policy
  • Signed asset return acknowledgments collected from all staff
  • Consent forms: full count verified, stored at institutional office, access log maintained
  • Salary register finalised and signed by all staff for the final payment
  • Final project summary shared with RO Team Lead: survey count, quality summary, key learnings
  • Wrap-up checklist signed by RA and filed with RO Team Lead
🗣️ Field Scenario — Pune, Maharashtra

The Situation: A women's SHG survey in Pune wrapped up fieldwork in September. The RA left for a conference the same day. The PA said he would "handle wrap-up." Four months later, a routine RO check found: 14 surveyors still had view access to the tracking sheet containing 800 respondent names and phone numbers. Three tablets had not been returned. The project phone's WhatsApp still contained 3 months of respondent coordination messages. No wrap-up checklist had been completed.

The project required a full wrap-up remediation, an IRB incident notification, and a formal meeting with the PI to explain the gaps. The PA was placed on a performance improvement plan. The RA's rating for the project noted "inadequate wrap-up oversight."

📌 Key Learning: Wrap-up is not "what happens after the interesting work." It is a critical phase that protects participants, protects the organisation, and protects your professional record. Build the checklist before fieldwork begins — and stay until it's done.
