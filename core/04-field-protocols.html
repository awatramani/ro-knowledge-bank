Your Daily Routine

The Field Day Protocol

A structured field day is not bureaucracy — it is quality control. Predictable routines catch problems early and keep teams coordinated across large, dispersed operations.

6:00–7:00 AM
Morning Prep
Review household list, charge tablet, confirm schedule with supervisor. Read back yesterday\ callbacks.
Do this before leaving your accommodation
7:00–12:00
Field Work Block 1
Priority interviews: target hard-to-reach respondents who are home in the morning (farmers, elderly).
Track every visit in real time — not from memory at day end
12:00–13:00
Midday Check-in
Brief team sync. Flag any blocked households, problem interviews, or skip logic issues encountered.
Do not skip check-ins — they catch problems the same day
13:00–17:00
Field Work Block 2
Afternoon interviews and first-attempt revisits for morning absences.
Schedule afternoon callbacks for those confirmed away in the morning
17:00–18:00
Close of Day
Final tracking sheet update. Sync SurveyCTO data to server. No exceptions.
Data not synced is data at risk — always sync before sleeping
18:00–19:00
Evening Debrief
Supervisor-led review of day\ work. Issues raised, callbacks planned, difficult cases reviewed.
Raise problems here — not the next morning when they are 24 hours old
Night
Rest & Prep
Tablet charged, tracking sheet updated, bag packed for tomorrow.
Your readiness tomorrow starts tonight
Safety Protocols

Personal Safety in the Field

No data point is worth your physical safety. These protocols are non-negotiable.

📍 Location Sharing

Share your daily field route with your supervisor before leaving. Check in at midday and on returning. Do not go off-route without notifying the team.

🚫 Go/No-Go Conditions

Do not conduct interviews during communal tensions, after dark in unfamiliar areas, or during dangerous weather. When in doubt, stop and call.

👥 Pair Protocol

In areas assessed as moderate-risk, work in pairs. Never enter a home alone if the respondent context feels unsafe. Your instinct is valid field data.

⚠️ Non-Negotiable Safety Rules
  • Never continue fieldwork during active communal or political unrest
  • Never enter a vehicle with unknown persons to reach a field location
  • Never share the household list or respondent data with local community leaders or officials
  • Never continue an interview if the respondent or a third party becomes threatening
  • Always have your supervisor\ number on speed dial
🔑 The Stop Principle

If at any point during fieldwork you feel unsafe — not just uncomfortable, but unsafe — you are authorised to stop, leave the location, and call your supervisor. You do not need permission to leave an unsafe situation. You do need to report it immediately.

Community Entry

Entering a Community Respectfully

How you enter a village shapes every interview that follows. A misstep in the first hour can close doors for the rest of the fieldwork week.

Community Leader asks:

"Who sent you? What are you going to do with this information?"

Surveyor responds:

"We are researchers from [organization]. We are conducting a survey to understand how households in this area are doing — on farming, income, health, and education. The information stays with the research team and is never shared with government, NGOs, or anyone else in the community. Participating is completely voluntary."

Managing Refusals

Handling Non-Participation with Professionalism

A refusal is not a failure — it is information. How you handle it determines whether the door stays open for a second attempt or closes permanently.

How Not to Handle Refusals
  • Arguing with the respondent about the value of the study
  • Suggesting that non-participation has negative consequences
  • Returning the same day after a clear "no"
  • Reporting a refusal without documenting the specific stated reason
How to Handle Refusals Well
  • Thank them for their time and leave without pressure
  • Document the exact words they used to decline
  • Leave a contact card if they change their mind
  • Consult supervisor before any second approach
