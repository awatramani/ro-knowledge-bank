Field Readiness › Field Readiness Overview

Every rupee spent on preparation during this phase saves five during execution. Too often, projects rush from training directly into full-scale launch. The first few days should be seen as input days—opportunities to find broken skip patterns, map missing villages, and correct communication flows. Field readiness is designed to prevent these failures before they scale.

✅ The Field Readiness Equation

Right People × Tested Instrument × Clear Processes × Working Systems × Live Monitoring = Field Ready

If any one multiplier is zero, the product is zero. A brilliant survey instrument with an inadequately trained team produces bad data. A well-trained team without GPS tracking produces unverifiable data. Field readiness requires all five.

🏛️ What does 'Field Ready' actually mean?

A project is only field-ready when these five pillars are validated through real-world testing:

Pillar What It Covers Validated Through
People Hiring timeline, quality, team structure, role clarity, bench strength Mock interviews, training evaluations, pilot performance
Instrument Survey logic, skip patterns, translation accuracy, response codes Pilot surveys with 25-40 diverse respondents
Processes Daily rhythms, reporting flows, escalation protocols, handovers 2-3 pilot days simulating real field conditions
Systems Tablets, SurveyCTO, GPS, data sync, attendance tracking Full end-to-end data flow test from field to server
Monitoring Supervision frameworks, quality dashboards, back-check logistics Daily review
🗣️ The ₹4.2 Lakh Lesson in Jharkhand

PA Neha was managing a 12-surveyor team for an education RCT in Ranchi. Under pressure, she skipped the pilot phase and launched directly after training. By Day 4, critical problems emerged: a broken skip pattern recorded phantom students, surveyors hadn't captured GPS, and daily debriefs lacked structure.

Cost: 340 surveys required re-visits (₹1.2 lakh), 2 weeks of delay, and ₹3 lakh in extended team rental. A 3-day pilot would have caught everything.
Lesson: 'Jaldi karna hai toh pehle theek se taiyaari karo' (If you want speed, first prepare properly).

The Field Readiness Journey

Field Readiness is a sequence of deliberate steps. The journey begins with hiring the right people and ends with a fully operational field machine that self-corrects in real time. Click through the sub-pages below for detailed playbooks.

1️⃣ Hiring Field Staff & Core Team

Your field team is your data collection engine. Every data point you analyse began as a conversation between a surveyor and a respondent. The quality of that conversation depends entirely on who you hire.

  • Sourcing: Strategies across team members.
  • Screening: Building the screening architecture.
  • Team Structure: Sizing, supervisor-to-surveyor ratios, and bench strength.
  • Validation: Any Field Staff getting a contract after training is assumed to be fit for the job based on your rigorous review of their skills.
🚩 The Cost of a Bad Hire One surveyor who fabricates 2 surveys per day across a 30-day deployment produces 60 fraudulent records. At ₹500/survey for back-check remediation, that single bad hire costs ₹30,000, plus weeks of team disruption and potential IRB complications.

2️⃣ Piloting and Pre-Launch Activities

Piloting is the single most undervalued activity in survey operations. It is a full dress rehearsal where you test the instrument, people, processes, and systems simultaneously.

✅ The 6 Pillars of a Strong Pilot
  1. Test with real respondents, not team members.
  2. Cover ALL demographic edge cases (joint families, migrants).
  3. Run the full daily cycle: briefing → field → debrief → sync.
  4. Populate your monitoring dashboard with pilot data.
  5. Time every survey — track the median, not the mean.
  6. Debrief every surveyor individually.
🗣️ The Skip Pattern Nobody Tested

During a hurried 1-day pilot in Muzaffarpur, the team tested only 5 surveys—all with nuclear families. On Day 7 of full launch, joint families with 8-12 members triggered a logic error.

Cost: 280 corrupted surveys, ₹1.8 lakh in re-survey costs, 11-day delay.
Lesson: Pilot with 25-40 respondents covering ALL demographic edge cases.

3️⃣ Survey Launch and Initial Days

The first two weeks are when habits form, norms solidify, and problems either get caught early or become entrenched. A project that establishes discipline in the first 14 days maintains it. A project that drifts rarely recovers.

✅ Teams That Invest 🚩 Teams That Skip
RA present in field for first 5 days RA visits field only in Week 3
Nightly debrief with each surveyor Debriefs happen 'when there's time'
First back-check by Day 5 First back-check in Week 3
Replacement surveyors on standby Attrition forces emergency hiring

"The first three days are for acclimatization and catching real-world travel logistics. Days 4-7 reveal who is fast and who is struggling. By Day 14, the survey rhythm either solidifies or breaks down entirely."

4️⃣ Google Maps to Track Productivity

A printed list of village names is not a field plan. A Google Map with colour-coded pins showing treatment arms, team assignments, travel routes, and completion status—that is a field plan.

  • Treatment Arms: Colour-coded pins prevent contamination.
  • Team Assignments: Visual workload balancing identifies travel inefficiencies.
  • Travel Routes: Plotted paths optimize fuel costs and reduce backtracking.
  • Completion Status: Pin styles change (hollow → filled) for real-time progress.
🗣️ How Maps Saved 3 Weeks in Udaipur

RA Meera initially assigned villages alphabetically. When mapped, she found Team A was crossing 3 tehsils daily (60km apart), while Team C was clustered within 8km. She reorganized geographically.

Result: Daily travel dropped from 45km to 18km per team. Saved ₹2.4 lakh in fuel and gave teams 1.5 extra survey hours daily.
Lesson: Always plot before you deploy. Geography is the primary planning variable.

5️⃣ Digital Attendance Tracking

Moving away from paper registers to automated attendance, leave management, and live productivity dashboards starting from Day 1 ensures absolute accountability and transparent payroll processing.

🛡️ The "Buffer" Rule

Hiring for the field is unique. You aren't just looking for competence; you are looking for resilience. In short-term projects, the biggest risk is "ghosting"—where staff vanish after training.

The 20% Rule: If you need 20 surveyors, train 25. If all 25 stay, you finish data collection early. If 5 drop out, your timeline is safe.

💡 Pro Tip: The Language Switch Don't just interview in one language. During the screening call, switch languages mid-sentence (e.g., English to Hindi/Tamil). If they stumble or panic, they will likely struggle with translation in the field.

Field Readiness › Piloting & Pre-Launch

The Human Dimension of Piloting

A pilot is not only a test of tools and protocols, but also an opportunity to understand the human realities of field implementation. The individuals who will eventually carry out large-scale data collection are the backbone of the process, and their ability to work effectively depends on the environment and conditions in which they operate.

This means assessing the physical demands and logistical constraints that field staff will encounter. Teams should take a critical view of travel distances, the complexity of routes, terrain challenges, and the impact of geographic spread. Long travel times, poor connectivity, unpredictable transport options, or difficult terrain can all impact data quality and enumerator well-being if not anticipated early.

Ultimately, the aim is to pre-empt fatigue, bottlenecks, and operational risks, laying the groundwork for a data collection phase that is not only efficient but also humane and sustainable.

Key Objectives of Piloting

Hover for methodology

🧠 Comprehension & Flow

Finalize and test the questionnaire across multiple modules. Assess clarity, flow, and skip logic specifically in the local language.

🎯 Respondent Burden

Evaluate the comfort level and burden on respondents. Understand the exact time required for each module under real field conditions.

🗺️ Logistical Friction

Assess real-life logistical challenges: travel times, location access, and realistic scheduling limits for enumerators.

🚦 Protocol Tuning

Fine-tune implementation protocols required for upcoming surveyor training and establish realistic field monitoring/HFC structures.

💬 Team Feedback Loop

Capture field team feedback during daily debriefs and rapidly absorb these insights into the final protocol updates.

🛡️ Ethical Integrity

Ensure ethical procedures and consent protocols actually work in practice without intimidating or confusing the respondent.

Operating Philosophy

Core Principles of the Crash Test

If your pilot does not break anything, you did not pilot hard enough.

Mimic Final Conditions

Pilot in the same type of setting where the main data collection will occur. Urban slums, remote villages, classrooms, or government offices all carry unique constraints.

Sample Smartly

Choose respondents across key categories (e.g., varying age, location, and literacy levels) to test the range and boundary cases of your instrument.

Pilot Everything

Test devices, translations, audio consents, the sampling process, supervisor debrief protocols, and physical consent tracking sheets.

Absorb & Adapt

Use daily debriefs to identify friction points. Involve the core team in every conversation and be willing to rewrite protocols based on field feedback.

Field Readiness › Survey Launch

For field teams and RAs, this is the moment to ensure everything is lined up, clearly communicated, and fully functional. The first 10 to 15 days are used to build momentum, establish unbreakable routines, and surface early operational lessons.

Key Pillars of a Successful Launch

🎯 1. Clarity for Field Staff

At launch, staff must have absolute clarity on roles, contracting details, daily routines, working conditions, and payment terms (stipend/DA frequency). There is no room for ambiguity.

Action: Distribute Field Manuals and R&R documents in advance. Review them extensively during training and reiterate them on launch morning.

Field Scenario A surveyor arrives on Day 1 confused about whether transport is reimbursed daily or weekly. They spend the first two hours arguing with the supervisor instead of conducting surveys. Clear payment terms prevent Day 1 friction.

📦 2. Logistics & Field Readiness

Every element of planning must be finalized: Start times, pickup points, whether taxi routes are fixed, lunch arrangements, emergency contacts, and device charging protocols.

Action: Document all of this in a Field Logistics SOP. Keep laminated hard copies inside every team vehicle.

Field Scenario The team reaches a remote village, but the tablets are at 15% battery because the charging protocol wasn't enforced the night before. The entire day is lost. Logistics dictate data flow.

🗣️ Reporting & Debriefing Systems

The launch week must introduce the culture of daily field reflection.

  • Decide who leads daily debriefs (usually SC or PA).
  • Use a Debrief Tracker to capture key themes.
  • Encourage open conversations: what’s working, what’s frustrating?
Pro Tip Make the first 5–7 debriefs mandatory and highly structured—this builds discipline and trust early on.

🚨 Monitoring & Escalation Setup

Finalize exactly how survey progress and emergencies will be tracked.

  • Share protocols for flagging: device failures, respondent refusals, or safety concerns.
  • Assign point persons for Attendance Logs, Logistics Tracking (PA/FM), and Backcheck Planning (SC/FM).

RA Launch Checklist & Tips

  • Create a launch kick-off pack: Include printed field plans, route maps, supervisor contacts, attendance formats, and team rosters.
  • Printer Access: Ensure at least one field team member has portable printer access or knows a local shop for emergency forms/logs.
  • Schedule Yourself: Work with RO to build a launch week schedule that includes heavy in-field time for you and the SC. Do not manage launch week from a desk.
  • The 3-Day Check-in: Conduct a short surveyor self-reflection after 3 days. Ask them directly: what’s working, what’s not?

📥 Download Dummy Dataset (CSV)

1. Preparing Your Dataset

Your map is only as good as your data. Before you touch Google My Maps, you must prepare a clean, well-structured dataset in Google Sheets[cite: 94]. Every field tracking dataset should follow this standard schema[cite: 97]:

Column Name Required? Description [cite: 99]
hh_id✅ YesUnique household identifier (e.g., JSM-0001)
latitude / longitude✅ YesGPS coords in decimal degrees (e.g., 26.753462)
treatment_status✅ YesTreatment or Control
biker_team✅ YesAssigned surveyor/biker ID (e.g., Biker-03)
survey_day✅ YesScheduled survey day (e.g., Day-01)
survey_status✅ YesPending / Completed / Refused / Revisit
🚩 The #1 Reason Maps Break If your treatment_status column contains 'Treatment', 'treatment', 'TREATMENT', and 'treat' — Google My Maps will create 4 separate categories instead of 2. Your color coding will break. Rule: Run a UNIQUE() check on every categorical column before importing[cite: 103].

2. Creating & Importing Your Map

Google My Maps is a free, browser-based tool that lets you create custom maps with pins, routes, and multiple layers[cite: 89]. Follow these exact steps to load your dummy data.

Step 1: Upload to Google Sheets First [cite: 127]

  1. Go to sheets.google.com → Blank spreadsheet.
  2. File → Import → Upload → Select dummy_hh_500_field_tracking.csv.
  3. Name the sheet: 'JSM_HH_Sample_Master'.
  4. Why? Google My Maps imports from Sheets more reliably than from raw CSVs.

Step 2: Create Map & Import [cite: 125, 128]

  1. Go to mymaps.google.com and click '+ CREATE A NEW MAP'.
  2. Name your map: 'JSM-RCT — Field Tracking Map — April 2026'[cite: 126].
  3. Click 'Import' in the Untitled layer → Select 'Google Sheets' tab → Choose your Master sheet.
  4. Position columns: Check 'latitude' and 'longitude'.
  5. Title column: Choose 'hh_id'. Click Finish.
🎨 AI Image Placeholder

A computer screen showing the Google My Maps import dialog box, with an arrow pointing to the "Google Drive" import option.

3. Styling & Color-Coding

The most critical visual distinction is Treatment vs. Control[cite: 130]. You cannot apply two color schemes to the same layer, so we will create distinct layers for different views[cite: 135].

Step 3: Color-Code Treatment Arms [cite: 132]

  1. In the layer panel, click 'Uniform style' → Change to 'Style by data column'.
  2. Select 'treatment_status'.
  3. Click the paint bucket next to 'Treatment' → Choose 🟢 GREEN (#34A853).
  4. Click the paint bucket next to 'Control' → Choose 🔴 RED (#EA4335).

Step 4: Create Team Assignment Layer [cite: 135]

  1. Click 'Add layer' in the left panel and name it 'Team Assignments'.
  2. Import the same Google Sheet into this new layer.
  3. Style by data column → 'biker_team'. Google will auto-assign 20 distinct colors.
🎨 AI Image Placeholder

A map interface showing a sea of green and red pins, with a user's cursor clicking the "paint bucket" tool on the sidebar.

4. Route Planning & The 3+3 Model

This is the most operationally powerful layer. For each day, you create a sub-layer showing only that day's targets for each biker[cite: 158]. We use the 3+3 Model: 3 primary targets and 3 buffer households to ensure no surveyor has a zero-productivity day[cite: 194].

Step 5: Filter and Import Daily Routes [cite: 159, 190, 191]

  1. In Google Sheets, create a new tab 'Day_01'.
  2. Use formula: =FILTER(Master!A:O, Master!J:J = "Day-01")
  3. In My Maps, 'Add layer' named 'Day 01 — Routes' and import this new tab.
  4. Style by survey_type: Primary = 🔵 Blue Circle, Buffer = 🟡 Yellow Diamond.

Step 6: Add Driving Directions [cite: 192]

  1. Click the 'Add directions' button (🔀) beneath the search bar.
  2. Set transport mode to 🏍️ Driving.
  3. Enter waypoints in order: Base Camp → Primary HH #1 → Primary HH #2 → Primary HH #3.
  4. Google will auto-calculate the route line. Change its color to match the biker's team color.
🎨 AI Image Placeholder

A map showing a defined route line connecting three blue circles (Primary targets), with three yellow diamonds (Buffers) clustered nearby.

5. Daily Tracking & Updates

A map that is not updated daily is a decoration, not a tool[cite: 203]. Every evening, the PA/SC performs a 15-minute map review to track progress and identify fraud[cite: 216].

Step 7: End-of-Day Status Update [cite: 210]

  1. Biker reports results: 'JSM-0041 Completed, JSM-0043 Refused, JSM-0044 Buffer Used'.
  2. SC opens the Google Sheet and updates the survey_status column accordingly.
  3. After all updates, REFRESH the Google My Maps browser tab. Pins will change color based on the new status[cite: 210, 211].
📱 The WhatsApp Screenshot Hack [cite: 207]

Most field staff don't open Google My Maps on their phones. Every morning, take a screenshot of each biker's daily route on your PC and send it to their WhatsApp. It takes 2 minutes per biker but eliminates the #1 field excuse: 'Mujhe pata nahi tha kahan jaana hai' (I didn't know where to go).

🎨 AI Image Placeholder

A smartphone displaying a WhatsApp chat where a supervisor has sent a clear screenshot of a map route with instructions to a surveyor.

6. Sharing & Self-Assessment

Once your map is built, you can share it securely or export it for PI presentations[cite: 242].

Step 8: Share & Export [cite: 244, 249]

  • Share: Click 'Share'. Give PA/SC 'Can edit' access. Give Surveyors 'Can view' access[cite: 244].
  • Export: Click the ⋮ (three dots) next to the map title → 'Export to KML/KMZ'. You can open this file in Google Earth for 3D terrain visualization[cite: 249].

  • Color-code pins by Treatment and Team
  • Filter and import a specific day's route
  • Add driving directions between waypoints
  • Update status in Sheets and refresh the map

4. Route Planning & The 3+3 Model

This is the most operationally powerful layer. For each day, you create a sub-layer showing only that day's targets for each biker[cite: 158]. We use the 3+3 Model: 3 primary targets and 3 buffer households to ensure no surveyor has a zero-productivity day[cite: 194].

Step 5: Filter and Import Daily Routes [cite: 159, 190, 191]

  1. In Google Sheets, create a new tab 'Day_01'.
  2. Use formula: =FILTER(Master!A:O, Master!J:J = "Day-01")
  3. In My Maps, 'Add layer' named 'Day 01 — Routes' and import this new tab.
  4. Style by survey_type: Primary = 🔵 Blue Circle, Buffer = 🟡 Yellow Diamond.

Step 6: Add Driving Directions [cite: 192]

  1. Click the 'Add directions' button (🔀) beneath the search bar.
  2. Set transport mode to 🏍️ Driving.
  3. Enter waypoints in order: Base Camp → Primary HH #1 → Primary HH #2 → Primary HH #3.
  4. Google will auto-calculate the route line. Change its color to match the biker's team color.

1. Preparing Your Dataset

Your map is only as good as your data. Before you touch Google My Maps, you must prepare a clean, well-structured dataset in Google Sheets[cite: 94]. Every field tracking dataset should follow this standard schema[cite: 97]:

Column Name Required? Description [cite: 99]
hh_id✅ YesUnique household identifier (e.g., JSM-0001)
latitude / longitude✅ YesGPS coords in decimal degrees (e.g., 26.753462)
treatment_status✅ YesTreatment or Control
biker_team✅ YesAssigned surveyor/biker ID (e.g., Biker-03)
survey_day✅ YesScheduled survey day (e.g., Day-01)
survey_status✅ YesPending / Completed / Refused / Revisit
🚩 The #1 Reason Maps Break If your treatment_status column contains 'Treatment', 'treatment', 'TREATMENT', and 'treat' — Google My Maps will create 4 separate categories instead of 2. Your color coding will break. Rule: Run a UNIQUE() check on every categorical column before importing[cite: 103].
