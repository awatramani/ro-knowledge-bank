Surveyor Basics & Research Foundations

Understanding why the study exists, how it works, and what your role demands
📘 Foundation Module Version 1.0 · 2026 ⏱ 20 min read
The Foundation

Why This Study Exists

New Surveyor asks:

"I have my tablet and I know the questions. Why do I need to understand why the study exists at all?"

Senior RA replies:

"Because when you understand why the evidence matters, you understand why every single data point matters. You are not filling in a form. You are generating scientific proof that governments use to decide where to spend money on the people who need it most."

What Is a Research Study?

A research study is a structured, systematic effort to answer a specific question about the real world. In the kind of work you will be doing, the question is almost always: Does this program actually work?

Governments and NGOs spend thousands of crores every year on programs designed to reduce poverty, improve education, and expand financial access. Without rigorous evidence, there is no way to know whether these programs are actually effective. Some that seem like they should work turn out to have no effect. Others cause unintended harm at scale.

Your role as a surveyor is to collect the evidence that separates what works from what does not. The standards are scientific — not administrative. Every question has a reason. Every protocol exists to protect the integrity of that evidence.

🔑 Key Principle

Every number you record, every response you capture, every GPS point you take is a brick in the wall of evidence that governments use to decide how to spend limited resources on the people who need help the most. If the brick is crooked, the wall is weak.

RCT Basics & J-PAL

How a Randomized Evaluation Works

The studies you work on use a method called a Randomized Controlled Trial (RCT). Think of it as a fair lottery. Before a program begins, eligible participants are randomly split into two groups:

🟢 Treatment Group

These participants receive the program being tested. Their outcomes are measured after a set period.

🔵 Control Group

These participants do not receive the program during the study. They continue with their normal lives.

🔑 Why Both Groups Matter Equally

Your surveys are the measurement. Without your data from both groups — collected with equal care and completeness — the entire design falls apart. This is why every single respondent, in both the treatment and control group, matters equally.

What Is J-PAL?

J-PAL (Abdul Latif Jameel Poverty Action Lab) is a global research network based at MIT. It supports researchers who run randomized evaluations to test whether social programs actually work — in education, health, agriculture, financial access, and more. J-PAL does not implement programs itself. It supports independent research teams to rigorously evaluate programs run by governments and NGOs.

Field Scenario: The Farmer Who Asks Why He Wasn't Chosen "My neighbor Ramesh told me his village is getting the new seed variety. Why wasn't our village chosen? Is it because we are smaller?"

Say: "The villages were chosen by a method similar to a lottery — a random selection. It has nothing to do with the size of your village. Your participation is extremely valuable regardless of which group your village is in."
Your Standards

Professional Conduct in the Field

You represent the study in the field. For the respondent, you are the study. Your appearance, language, punctuality, and behavior form their entire impression of the research.

Appearance & Communication

  • Dress modestly and cleanly. Avoid branded clothing, expensive watches, or accessories that signal wealth or authority.
  • Wear your identification badge visibly at all times. Without it, you are a stranger asking personal questions.
  • Remove footwear before entering homes where this is the cultural norm. Do not wait to be asked.
  • Always greet first. Use local greetings — Namaste, As-salamu Alaikum, Vanakkam — whatever is appropriate. Never begin with a request.
  • Never use technical jargon: "randomized evaluation," "control group," "sample frame" — these words create confusion and suspicion.
  • Never argue with a respondent. Record what they said. Your job is to document their response, not correct it.
  • Never express opinions about politics, religion, caste, or government schemes. You are professionally neutral at all times.

🔑 The Golden Rule

When in doubt, do not guess. Call your supervisor. A wrong decision that is reported immediately can almost always be corrected. A wrong decision that is hidden cannot.

Data Integrity: What It Looks Like

✅ What Good Looks Like

  • Mark household "absent" when truly absent — even if it means returning tomorrow.
  • Record "Don't Know" when the respondent genuinely doesn't know. Never guess.
  • Report questionnaire problems to your supervisor even if it slows daily progress.
  • Tell a respondent honestly you don't know an answer rather than making something up.

❌ Termination Offenses — No Exceptions

  • Data fabrication: Filling in responses without conducting the interview. This is fraud.
  • Data falsification: Changing an answer to pass a validation check or "make more sense."
  • Recording a wrong respondent under the listed respondent's ID.
  • Sharing respondent data with anyone outside the research team.
  • Using the survey device for personal purposes: social media, calls, photos.
The Research Calendar

Where You Fit in the Study Timeline

Every research study follows a structured timeline. Timing is not just an administrative convenience — it is a scientific requirement. Baseline data must be collected before the program starts. Endline data must be collected after sufficient time has passed. If either window is missed, the evidence cannot be generated.

The Deadline That Cannot Move It is the last week of the baseline survey. Your team has completed 85% of the target. The monsoon starts in three days, after which roads in Bihar will be impassable for weeks.

What happens: Teams reorganize, hours extend, assignments are redistributed. What does NOT happen: Nobody fabricates data. The deadline creates pressure — managed through planning, never by compromising data quality.

Navigating the Field

Understanding the Implementing Partner

In most studies, the intervention being evaluated is run by an implementing partner — an NGO, a government department, or a community organization. The research team evaluates the partner's program; it does not deliver it. Respondents will often confuse you with the partner.

🔑 What to Always Say

"I am from [research organization]. We are a research team conducting a survey to understand how communities like yours are doing. We are not from the government and we are not distributing any program benefits."

Trick Questions: Government Partner

Trick Question — Government Context "Are you from the department office? Can you check why I haven't received my scheme payment?"

Say: "I am from [research organization], a separate research team. I am not from the government office and I cannot access any scheme records. Please contact the department office directly for payment issues."
Trick Question — NGO Context "The NGO worker accompanied you to my house. Does that mean I have to answer your questions?"

Say: "Not at all. This interview is completely separate from the NGO's work. Participation is entirely your choice and has no impact on anything the NGO does for you."
Common Mistakes
  • Assuming NGO presence means respondents must cooperate.
  • Sharing respondent lists with partner field staff.
  • Implying participation leads to program benefits.
  • Letting partner staff sit in during interviews.
What Good Looks Like
  • Clearly state you are from the research team, not the partner.
  • Politely decline data sharing requests and escalate.
  • Make the voluntary nature of consent unmistakably clear.
  • Always interview one-on-one with the respondent.

Study Basics

You must be able to answer these before your first interview. If you cannot, ask your supervisor during training — not in the field.

✓
I can explain what this study is trying to find out, in one or two plain sentences.
✓
I understand what a randomized evaluation is and can explain treatment vs. control groups in simple language.
✓
I know whether this is a baseline, midline, or endline survey and what that means for my work.
✓
I know the fieldwork timeline: start date, end date, and daily targets.
✓
I know the target population and eligibility criteria for this study.

⚠️ The Trap

Going into the field without understanding why the study exists. If you don't understand the purpose, you cannot explain it during consent — and that's where trust is built or lost.

Professional Conduct

Your conduct in the field defines the quality of the data. These are not guidelines — they are requirements.

✓
I know who my supervisor is, how to contact them, and that I must report to them daily without exception.
✓
I can list situations where I make independent decisions vs. situations where I must escalate.
✓
I understand that both treatment and control group respondents are equally important and must receive equal care.
✓
I will never speculate about study findings or share preliminary impressions with respondents.

Implementing Partner

You must be clear about who you are and who you are not — especially when respondents confuse you with the implementing partner.

✓
I can name the implementing partner, describe what they do, and clearly explain my independent role.
✓
I know how to respond when a respondent asks if I am from the government or if the survey is linked to benefits.
✓
I know I must never share respondent data or lists with partner field staff, and I know to escalate if asked.
✓
I will report immediately if partner staff imply that survey participation leads to program benefits.

⚠️ The Trap

Letting NGO or government staff sit in during interviews. Their presence — even silently — changes what the respondent says. Always interview one-on-one.

Data Integrity

These are the non-negotiables. Any violation is grounds for immediate termination.

✓
I understand that data fabrication — filling in responses without conducting the interview — is fraud.
✓
I understand that changing a respondent's answer for any reason is data falsification — a termination offense.
✓
I know that the quality of my data has a direct impact on real policy decisions that affect real people.
🔑 Closing Principle

You are not just asking questions. You are generating evidence that governments will use to make decisions about where to invest scarce resources. Every survey you complete with integrity brings us one step closer to knowing what actually works.
