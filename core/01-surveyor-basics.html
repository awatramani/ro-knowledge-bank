Understanding why the study exists, how it works, and what your role demands

The Foundation

Why This Study Exists

Every number you record, every response you capture, every GPS point you take is a brick in the wall of evidence that governments use to decide how to spend limited resources on the people who need help the most.

New Surveyor asks:

"I have my tablet and I know the questions. Why do I need to understand why the study exists at all?"

Senior RA replies:

"Because when you understand why the evidence matters, you understand why every single data point matters. You are not filling in a form. You are generating scientific proof that governments use to decide where to spend money on the people who need it most."

What Is a Research Study?

A research study is a structured, systematic effort to answer a specific question about the real world. In the kind of work you will be doing, the question is almost always: Does this program actually work?

Governments and NGOs spend thousands of crores every year on programs designed to reduce poverty, improve education, and expand financial access. Without rigorous evidence, there is no way to know whether these programs are actually effective. Some that seem like they should work turn out to have no effect. Others cause unintended harm at scale.

Your role as a surveyor is to collect the evidence that separates what works from what does not. The standards are scientific — not administrative. Every question has a reason. Every protocol exists to protect the integrity of that evidence.

🔑 Key Principle

You are not just asking questions. You are generating evidence that governments will use to make decisions about where to invest scarce resources. Every survey you complete with integrity brings us one step closer to knowing what actually works.

What You Must Know About Your Study Before Day One

Before your first interview, you must be able to answer every question in the table below. If you cannot, ask your supervisor during training — not in the field.

Question You Must Answer Why It Matters
What is the research question?You need to explain the study's purpose during consent. If you don't understand it yourself, you can't explain it to anyone else.
What is the intervention being evaluated?Respondents will ask about the program. You need to know what it is without promising or promoting it.
Who is the implementing partner?Respondents will ask who is behind the survey. Name the organization accurately and know their role.
What is the target population?This defines who you survey. Getting this wrong means surveying the wrong people and corrupting the sample.

What are the daily and weekly targets?Targets help you manage time — but they must never compromise quality. A fabricated survey is worse than an incomplete one.
⚠️ Never Do This
  • Go into the field without understanding why the study exists or what it is trying to measure.
  • Tell respondents they "will definitely receive the benefit" or were "specially selected."
  • Treat the control group as less important. Both groups are equally critical.
  • Speculate about study findings or share preliminary impressions with community members.
RCT Basics & J-PAL

How a Randomized Evaluation Works

The studies you work on use a Randomized Controlled Trial — the gold standard for establishing whether a program actually caused a change in people's lives.

Think of it as a fair lottery. Before a program begins, eligible participants — villages, households, schools — are randomly split into two groups:

🟢 Treatment Group

These participants receive the program being tested. Their outcomes are measured after a set period.

🔵 Control Group

These participants do not receive the program during the study. They continue with their normal lives.

Because assignment is random — like a coin flip or a computer lottery — both groups are, on average, identical in every way before the program starts. The only difference is that one group received the program and the other did not. This makes it possible to measure the program's true effect.

🔑 Why Both Groups Matter Equally

Your surveys are the measurement. Without your data from both groups — collected with equal care and completeness — the entire design falls apart. Every single respondent, in both the treatment and control group, matters equally. If you survey the treatment group more thoroughly, the comparison becomes invalid.

What Is J-PAL?

J-PAL (Abdul Latif Jameel Poverty Action Lab) is a global research network based at MIT. It supports researchers who run randomized evaluations to test whether social programs actually work — in education, health, agriculture, financial access, and more. J-PAL does not implement programs itself. It supports independent research teams to rigorously evaluate programs run by governments and NGOs.

Field Scenario: The Farmer Who Asks Why He Wasn't Chosen "My neighbor Ramesh told me his village is getting the new seed variety. Why wasn't our village chosen? Is it because we are smaller?"

Correct response: "The villages were chosen by a method similar to a lottery — a random selection. It has nothing to do with the size of your village. Your participation is extremely valuable regardless of which group your village is in."
Your Standards

Professional Conduct & Data Integrity

You represent the study in the field. For the respondent, you are the study. Your appearance, language, punctuality, and behavior form their entire impression of the research.

Appearance & Communication

  • Dress modestly and cleanly. Avoid branded clothing or accessories that signal wealth or authority.
  • Wear your identification badge visibly at all times.
  • Remove footwear before entering homes where this is the cultural norm. Do not wait to be asked.
  • Always greet first. Use local greetings — Namaste, As-salamu Alaikum, Vanakkam. Never begin with a request.
  • Never use technical jargon: "randomized evaluation," "control group," "sample frame" — these create confusion and suspicion.
  • Never argue with a respondent. Record what they said. Your job is to document, not correct.
  • Never express opinions about politics, religion, caste, or government schemes. You are professionally neutral at all times.

Decide vs. Escalate

One of the most important skills a surveyor must develop is knowing the boundary between independent judgment and mandatory escalation.

🔑 The Golden Rule

When in doubt, do not guess. Call your supervisor. A wrong decision that is reported immediately can almost always be corrected. A wrong decision that is hidden will eventually be discovered — and it will be much harder to fix.

Data Integrity: What It Looks Like

✅ What Good Looks Like

  • Mark household "absent" when truly absent — even if it means returning tomorrow.
  • Record "Don't Know" when the respondent genuinely doesn't know. Never guess.
  • Report questionnaire problems to your supervisor even if it slows daily progress.
  • Tell a respondent honestly you don't know an answer rather than making something up.

❌ Termination Offenses

  • Data fabrication: Filling in responses without conducting the interview. This is fraud.
  • Data falsification: Changing an answer to pass a validation check.
  • Recording a wrong respondent under the listed respondent's ID.
  • Sharing respondent data with anyone outside the research team.
  • Using the survey device for personal purposes: calls, social media, photos.

Where You Fit in the Study Timeline

Timing is not just an administrative convenience — it is a scientific requirement. If a survey window is missed, the evidence cannot be generated.

Study Design

Phase 2
Training
Surveyors trained on protocol, CAPI tools, and ethical guidelines.
Your entry point. Pay maximum attention. Ask every question.
Phase 3
Pilot / Pre-Test
Small-scale test of questionnaire and field protocols before full launch.
Your feedback directly improves the final instrument.
Phase 4
Baseline Survey
Data collection before the intervention begins. Measures the starting point.
First major data collection. Accuracy here is non-negotiable.
Phase 5
Intervention Period
Program implemented in treatment areas. Control group continues normally.
May conduct monitoring surveys or process documentation.
Phase 6
Midline Survey
Interim data collection to measure early effects.
Track same respondents from baseline. Tracking protocols critical.
Phase 7
Endline Survey
Final data collection after the intervention has had time to work.
Most consequential round. Every baseline respondent must be found.
Phase 8
Analysis & Dissemination
Team analyzes data and shares findings with policymakers.
Your data may inform policy at state or national level.
The Deadline That Cannot Move It is the last week of the baseline survey. Your team has completed 85% of the target. The monsoon starts in three days, after which roads will be impassable. The intervention begins immediately after.

What happens: Teams reorganize, hours extend, assignments are redistributed. What does NOT happen: Nobody fabricates data. Deadlines create pressure — managed through planning, never by compromising data quality.
Navigating the Field

Understanding the Implementing Partner

In most studies, the intervention is run by an implementing partner — an NGO or government department. You evaluate their program; you do not deliver it. Respondents will often confuse you with them.

🔑 What to Always Say

"I am from [research organization]. We are a research team conducting a survey to understand how communities like yours are doing. We are not from the government and we are not distributing any program benefits."

Handling Trick Questions: Government Partner

Trick Question — Government Context "Are you from the department office? Can you check why I haven't received my scheme payment?"

Say: "I am from [research organization], a separate research team. I am not from the government office and I cannot access any scheme records. Please contact the department office directly."
Trick Question — NGO Context "The NGO worker accompanied you to my house. Does that mean I have to answer your questions?"

Say: "Not at all. This interview is completely separate from the NGO's work. Participation is entirely your choice and has no impact on anything the NGO does for you."

Partner Boundaries — What Good Looks Like

Common Mistakes
  • Assuming NGO presence means respondents must cooperate.
  • Sharing respondent lists with partner field staff.
  • Implying participation leads to program benefits.
  • Letting partner staff sit in during interviews.
What Good Looks Like
  • Clearly state you are from the research team, not the partner.
  • Politely decline data sharing requests and escalate.
  • Make the voluntary nature of consent unmistakably clear.
  • Always interview one-on-one with the respondent.
