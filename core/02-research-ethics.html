Surveyor Basics & Research Foundations

Understanding why the study exists, how it works, and what your role demands
📘 Foundation Module Version 1.0 · 2026 ⏱ 20 min read
The Foundation

Why This Study Exists

Every number you record, every response you capture, every GPS point you take is a brick in the wall of evidence that governments use to decide how to spend limited resources on the people who need help the most.

New Surveyor asks:

"I have my tablet and I know the questions. Why do I need to understand why the study exists at all?"

Senior RA replies:

"Because when you understand why the evidence matters, you understand why every single data point matters. You are not filling in a form. You are generating scientific proof."

What Is a Research Study?

A research study is a structured, systematic effort to answer a specific question about the real world. In the kind of work you will be doing, the question is almost always: Does this program actually work?

Governments spend thousands of crores every year on programs designed to reduce poverty. Without rigorous evidence, there is no way to know whether these programs are effective. Your role is to collect the evidence that separates what works from what does not. Every protocol exists to protect the integrity of that evidence.

What You Must Know Before Day One

Before your first interview, you must be able to answer every question below. If you cannot, ask your supervisor.

Question You Must Answer Why It Matters
What is the research question?You need to explain the study's purpose during consent.
What is the intervention?Respondents will ask about the program. You need to know what it is without promoting it.
What is the target population?Getting this wrong means surveying the wrong people and corrupting the sample.
Is this baseline, midline, or endline?Determines what the data will be compared against.
What are the daily targets?Targets help manage time — but they must never compromise quality.
Methodology

How a Randomized Evaluation Works

The studies you work on use an RCT — the gold standard for establishing whether a program actually caused a change.

Think of it as a fair lottery. Before a program begins, eligible participants are randomly split into two groups:

🟢 Treatment Group

These participants receive the program being tested. Their outcomes are measured after a set period.

🔵 Control Group

These participants do not receive the program during the study. They continue with their normal lives.

Because assignment is random, both groups are identical in every way before the program starts. The only difference is that one group received the program and the other did not. This makes it possible to measure the program's true effect.

🔑 Why Both Groups Matter Equally

Your surveys are the measurement. Without your data from both groups — collected with equal care — the entire design falls apart. If you survey the treatment group more thoroughly, the comparison becomes invalid.

Field Scenario "Why Wasn't I Chosen?"

"My neighbor Ramesh told me his village is getting the new seed variety. Why wasn't our village chosen?"

Say: "The villages were chosen by a method similar to a lottery — a random selection. Your participation is extremely valuable regardless of which group your village is in."

Your Standards

Professional Conduct & Data Integrity

You represent the study in the field. For the respondent, you are the study. Your behavior forms their entire impression of the research.

Decide vs. Escalate

One of the most important skills a surveyor must develop is knowing the boundary between independent judgment and mandatory escalation.

⚠️ Termination Offenses
  • Data fabrication: Filling in responses without conducting the interview.
  • Data falsification: Changing an answer to pass a validation check.
  • Recording a wrong respondent under the listed respondent's ID.
  • Sharing respondent data with anyone outside the research team.
The Research Calendar

Where You Fit in the Timeline

Timing is not just an administrative convenience — it is a scientific requirement. If a survey window is missed, the evidence cannot be generated.

Phase 1
Study Design
Research question, sampling, and approvals finalized.
Phase 2
Training & Pilot
You are trained and conduct test interviews. Your feedback improves the final instrument.
Phase 3
Baseline Survey
Data collected before the program begins. Accuracy here is non-negotiable.
Phase 4
Intervention
Program implemented in treatment areas.
Phase 5
Endline Survey
Final data collection. Every baseline respondent must be found. Tracking is critical.
Navigating the Field

Understanding the Implementing Partner

In most studies, the intervention is run by an implementing partner — an NGO or government department. You evaluate their program; you do not deliver it. Respondents will often confuse you with them.

🔑 What to Always Say

"I am from [research organization]. We are a research team conducting a survey to understand how communities like yours are doing. We are not from the government and we are not distributing any program benefits."

Handling Trick Questions: Government Partner

Trick Question — Government Context "Are you from the department office? Can you check why I haven't received my scheme payment?"

Say: "I am from [research organization], a separate research team. I am not from the government office and I cannot access any scheme records. Please contact the department office directly."
Trick Question — NGO Context "The NGO worker accompanied you to my house. Does that mean I have to answer your questions?"

Say: "Not at all. This interview is completely separate from the NGO's work. Participation is entirely your choice and has no impact on anything the NGO does for you."

Partner Boundaries — What Good Looks Like

Common Mistakes
  • Assuming NGO presence means respondents must cooperate.
  • Sharing respondent lists with partner field staff.
  • Implying participation leads to program benefits.
  • Letting partner staff sit in during interviews.
What Good Looks Like
  • Clearly state you are from the research team, not the partner.
  • Politely decline data sharing requests and escalate.
  • Make the voluntary nature of consent unmistakably clear.
  • Always interview one-on-one with the respondent.
