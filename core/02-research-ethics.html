Why Ethics Matters

The Foundation: What Human Subjects Research Means

Every person you interview is a human subject. That is not a bureaucratic label — it is a legal and ethical designation that comes with obligations the research team must honour, and that you as a data collector must uphold in every interaction.

New Enumerator asks:

"I'm just asking questions. What does any of this ethics stuff have to do with my job?"

Field Coordinator replies:

"You are the only person from the research team that the respondent will ever meet. Everything they think about this study — whether it is trustworthy, whether their information is safe, whether they will participate at all — comes from you. You are not just asking questions. You are the ethics of this study, in person."

What Is "Research"?

US Federal guidelines (45 CFR 46) define research as "a systematic investigation, including research development, testing and evaluation, designed to develop or contribute to generalizable knowledge." In practice, this includes every formal interview, every questionnaire, and every household listing activity you conduct for J-PAL South Asia.

Who Is a Human Subject?

A human subject is any living individual from whom a researcher obtains data through interaction or from individually identifiable information. This means every respondent in your assignment list is a human subject — with full rights, protections, and the legal standing to consent or refuse.

You Are an Ambassador

The data collector is the face of the research. Participants often never meet the PI, the RA, or J-PAL. They meet you. Your conduct, tone, and professionalism are the study's reputation. A participant who feels respected provides honest data; one who feels coerced or confused does not — or worse, simply complies without true consent.

The Belmont Report

Three Core Ethical Principles

Published in 1979, the Belmont Report established the ethical framework for all human subjects research. Every protocol J-PAL uses traces back to these three principles.

🤝
Respect for Persons
Honour every individual's right to decide

Individuals must be treated as autonomous agents — free to make decisions about their own participation. Those with diminished autonomy (children, the cognitively impaired) are owed additional protection. In the field: never pressure, never assume, always ask.

⚖️
Beneficence
Do good. Minimise harm.

Research should maximise potential benefits and minimise risks — physical, psychological, social, and privacy-related. In the field: your job is to collect data in a way that never leaves the respondent worse off than before you arrived.

🏛️
Justice
Fair selection. Equitable burden.

The benefits and burdens of research must be distributed fairly. Vulnerable or disadvantaged populations must not be targeted simply because they are easy to access. In the field: every eligible respondent deserves the same quality of interaction.

How These Principles Look on the Ground

🤝 Respect in Action A respondent from the control group says: "My neighbour was told he will get a solar lamp. Why am I wasting my time with this?"

Say: "I understand that feels unfair. I want to be honest with you — I'm not involved in who receives the programme. But your answers are just as important. Without responses from people like you, we can't know what changes and what doesn't. Would you be willing to continue?"
⚖️ Beneficence in Action During a health survey, a respondent begins crying and discloses something deeply personal that is outside your questionnaire.

Do: Pause the survey. Acknowledge what they shared. Do not probe further. Give them time. If they are in distress, note it in your tracker and report to your supervisor immediately after the visit. Do not skip this step.
⚠️ The Three Violations That End Contracts
  • Coercion: Pressuring a respondent to answer, implying negative consequences for refusal, or offering promises you are not authorised to make.
  • Fabrication: Recording responses without conducting the interview. This is research fraud. It is grounds for immediate termination and may have legal consequences.
  • Confidentiality breach: Sharing respondent data with anyone — including the implementing partner's field staff, village leaders, or family members — who is not authorised to see it.
The Consent Process

Informed Consent: More Than a Signature

Informed consent is not a formality. It is an ongoing process that begins before the first question and continues for the entire duration of your interaction with each respondent. A signature on a form does not mean consent is complete.

What "Informed" Actually Means

True consent requires the respondent to understand what they are agreeing to. This means your explanation must be in plain language they can follow, not a rapid reading of the consent form. If a participant cannot explain back to you — in their own words — what the study is about and that they can leave at any time, you have not yet achieved informed consent.

Consent Is Ongoing

Consent does not end when the form is signed. At every subsequent visit — and sometimes during a single long interview — the participant may need to be reminded of their rights. Watch for signs of discomfort: crossed arms, averted eyes, short answers where you expected more. These are non-verbal signals to slow down.

🔑 The GIST Rule

People being approached to join the study should be provided correct and factual information. If it is observed during interaction that the participant's body language is uncomfortable — even if they have not said anything — this must be reported to team leaders or the survey operations head on the project. Body language is data too.

The 7 Components of a Valid Consent

Every consent interaction — whether verbal (phone surveys) or written (in-person surveys) — must cover all seven of these components.

Verbal vs. Written Consent

📞 Verbal Consent (Phone/Remote)

Used for telephonic and remote surveys. The enumerator reads the consent script aloud and the respondent's verbal agreement is recorded. The fact that verbal consent was given and the date/time must be logged in SurveyCTO. Verbal consent carries the same legal weight as written — do not rush through it.

✍️ Written Consent (In-Person)

Used for in-person surveys. Two copies are signed — one is retained by the organisation, one is given to the respondent. If the respondent cannot write, a thumbprint is acceptable under most IRB protocols. Check your project-specific consent form for exact instructions.

Privacy, PII & Confidentiality

Understanding Private Information

Privacy and confidentiality are two distinct things. Privacy is the respondent's right to control their own information. Confidentiality is your obligation as a data collector to protect the information they share with you. Both apply from the moment you arrive at the household to long after you submit the survey.

The Key Distinction

🔒 Privacy The respondent's right to control who observes them, what information they share, and in what context. It must be protected before, during, and after the consent process. Asking health questions in earshot of neighbours violates the respondent's privacy — even if they do not object.
🛡️ Confidentiality Your obligation to protect information the respondent has shared. Confidentiality agreements cover: what identifiers are retained, how data is stored and encrypted, and who has access. It begins the moment the respondent shares anything — including during the consent process itself.

What Is Personal Identifiable Information (PII)?

PII is any information that — alone or in combination with other data — can identify a specific individual. The risk is not just obvious identifiers like name or phone number. It is combinations: a village name, a caste, a disability, and an occupation together may uniquely identify one person in a small community.

🔴 Always PII — Never record unless required by protocol

  • Full name
  • Phone number / email address
  • Government ID numbers (Aadhaar, voter ID)
  • GPS coordinates linked to a named individual
  • Account numbers
  • Full-face photographs
  • Biometric data
  • Precise home address

⚪ Not PII on Its Own — But can become PII in combination

  • Village / district name
  • Gender
  • Age range (not exact date of birth)
  • Occupation category
  • Education level
  • Household size
  • Religion / caste (context-dependent)
  • Treatment group assignment
⚠️ The Combination Risk — A Real Field Scenario
  • You are conducting a survey in a village of 200 households. Your data shows: female respondent, aged 45–50, widowed, with a visual impairment, in the treatment group, near the primary school. That combination may identify exactly one person in the village.
  • Rule: Never discuss respondent details with other respondents, village leaders, NGO staff, or anyone outside the authorised study team. Even casually. Even without names.
  • Tablets: Your data collection app is password-protected. Never share your password. Never let others scroll through submitted forms. Report a lost or stolen tablet immediately.

Protecting Privacy in Practice

✅ What You Must Do

  • Request a private space before asking sensitive questions
  • Allow the respondent to choose where the interview takes place
  • Ask curious onlookers to give you space — respectfully
  • Submit data the same day before leaving the field
  • Store paper forms in a locked container immediately after use
  • Report any breach or near-miss to your supervisor the same day

❌ What You Must Never Do

  • Discuss one respondent's answers with another respondent
  • Share the household list or respondent names with NGO partners
  • Leave your tablet unlocked and unattended in the field
  • Allow implementing partner staff to sit in on interviews
  • Screenshot or photograph completed survey forms
  • Use respondent contact numbers for any purpose other than survey follow-up
Special Protections

Vulnerable Populations

Some respondents require additional care, modified consent procedures, or specialised communication approaches. Failing to recognise vulnerability and adapt accordingly is an ethical violation — even if no harm was intended.

Who Is Considered Vulnerable?

👶

Children & Minors

Cannot provide full independent consent. Parental/guardian consent required. Child's own assent also required (at age specified in protocol). Watch for fatigue and distress.

👴

Elderly Individuals

May have memory, hearing, or health limitations. Adapt pace, volume, and question complexity. More time needed for consent comprehension.

🧠

Cognitively Impaired

Limited capacity for decision-making. Consent must come from an authorised caregiver. Data collector must still respect the individual's comfort and signals.

🤲

Persons with Disabilities

Hearing, vision, or mobility impairments require adapted approaches. Pause and notify supervisor if no accessible format is available. Do not conduct interview without appropriate accommodation.

🏘️

Economically Disadvantaged

May feel obligated to participate due to fear of losing benefits. Extra care needed to ensure voluntary participation is genuine. Never imply survey is linked to programme eligibility.

🌿

Tribal / Ethnic Minorities

Distinct cultural norms around privacy, authority, and gender interaction. Must anticipate and adapt. Community gatekeeping may also affect voluntary participation.

🤰

Pregnant Women

Unique health considerations. Certain question topics (foetal health, income stress) may cause distress. Allow breaks and extra exit points.

📍

Persons in Custodial Settings

Prisoners, shelter residents, or institutionalised individuals have constrained autonomy. Voluntary participation is especially difficult to guarantee. Usually requires full board IRB review.

🔑 The Assent Rule for Children

When research involves children, two agreements are required: parental or guardian consent (a legal requirement) AND the child's own assent — their age-appropriate agreement to participate. If a child shows reluctance, discomfort, or distress, the interview must stop — even if the parent has consented. The child's wellbeing takes precedence over data collection targets at all times.

Safeguarding Protocols for Vulnerable Respondents

✅ Adapted Approach

  • Follow your project's specific protocol for vulnerable groups
  • Use child-friendly, simple language for minors
  • Have a support person present if the participant needs assistance
  • Identify and report any signs of distress, abuse, or neglect immediately
  • Slow down and check comprehension more frequently
  • Allow more time and more exit points throughout the interview

❌ What Must Never Happen

  • Interviewing a minor without parental consent and child assent
  • Continuing when a child expresses distress, even if parents say "it's fine"
  • Using complex or technical language with individuals with limited literacy
  • Asking a vulnerable person to answer in front of others who may judge them
  • Prioritising survey completion speed over the individual's comfort
  • Assuming someone's cognitive capacity based on age or appearance
The Science Depends on You

Data Integrity: Your Most Important Obligation

Data integrity is the ethical backbone of research. Wrong data does not just produce a wrong number — it produces a wrong policy recommendation that affects the lives of real people who have nothing to do with your survey.

What Constitutes a Data Integrity Violation?

Data integrity violations range from deliberate fraud to small, unreported mistakes. All are serious. All must be escalated to your supervisor. The three categories are:

Fabrication Recording responses without conducting the interview. The most severe violation — grounds for immediate termination and potential legal consequences.
Falsification Changing a respondent's actual answer — for any reason, including to "correct" what seems like an error. Never change an answer after recording it without supervisor guidance.
Deviation Any departure from approved study procedures — question wording, skip patterns, sampling — that is not reported to the supervisor and potentially the IRB.

Data Storage & Protection Rules

📱 Digital Data

  • Password-protect your tablet — do not share the password
  • Submit data before leaving the field each day
  • Do not store respondent PII in personal phone contacts or WhatsApp
  • Report a lost or stolen device immediately — same day
  • Never transfer data via personal email or unapproved channels

📄 Paper Data

  • Keep completed forms with you — never leave them unattended in the field
  • Hand forms only to the person designated as responsible for safe storage
  • Store in locked iron trunks or secured cabinets — not NGO partner offices
  • Do not photograph or scan paper forms without authorisation
  • Consent forms and questionnaires must be stored separately if required by protocol
Enumerator says:

"I made a mistake on one response — I entered Option 2 but the respondent said Option 3. I already submitted. Should I just fix it quietly?"

Correct response:

No. Report the error to your field supervisor immediately. Note the household ID, the question number, and exactly what the respondent said. Your supervisor will determine whether a back-check visit is needed. Attempting to alter submitted data without authorisation is a protocol deviation — even when done with good intentions.

Real Incidents — Real Consequences

Case Studies from the Field

These case studies are drawn directly from J-PAL South Asia's Human Subjects Certification training materials. Each one describes a real type of incident — with analysis of the violations and the lessons that must be learned.

1
Ethical Conduct & Privacy — Shyam Enters Without Waiting
▼
The Incident

Shyam was assigned to survey a respondent in Kosi village. He had spoken to the respondent by phone minutes earlier — the respondent confirmed his availability and was friendly. When Shyam arrived, he did not knock or wait. He walked in, reasoning that the phone call had established enough rapport and that waiting would waste time.

Ethical Violations

Entering someone's home without being invited crosses a fundamental privacy boundary. A household is not a public space. Even if the respondent seemed comfortable on the phone, physical entry into a home requires explicit invitation. Shyam's assumption — that prior friendly contact equals consent to enter — is exactly the kind of reasoning that erodes respondent trust.

The Lesson

Always knock and wait to be invited in. Once inside, let the respondent choose where the interview takes place — inside or outside, in one room or another. The right to decide the interview environment belongs to the respondent, not the surveyor.

2
Expedited Consent — Shamika Rushes the Form
▼
The Incident

Shamika, an experienced surveyor, was under pressure to meet daily targets. She adopted a strategy of rapidly reading the consent form aloud and immediately asking for a signature, without pausing for questions. Several respondents later expressed confusion about what they had agreed to. Some said they felt rushed and regretted not asking questions before signing.

Consequences

Participants had limited understanding of the study's purpose and potential risks. Rushing the consent process is a direct violation of the principle of informed consent. Some participants withdrew from follow-up surveys. The trust that Shamika had built over previous study waves was damaged in a single field season.

The Lesson

Daily targets are real pressures. But time saved during consent is never worth the cost. Participants must be given adequate time to ask questions. A signature without comprehension is not consent — it is a piece of paper. The ethical and long-term operational cost of rushing consent always exceeds the short-term time saving.

3
Privacy & Confidentiality Violation — Health Questions in Front of Neighbours
▼
The Incident

Shamika was conducting a health survey and proceeded to ask sensitive health history questions in the presence of the respondent's neighbours, who had gathered to watch. The project supervisor arrived and, seeing the situation, asked the neighbours to leave. Shamika did not proactively address the privacy issue.

Two Violations, Not One

The obvious violation: asking sensitive health questions in front of others compromised the respondent's privacy and the accuracy of their answers. The less obvious violation: the supervisor asking the neighbours to leave was disrespectful to the community — an authoritative gesture that could damage future field operations in the area. The correct approach would have been to ask the respondent privately if they would prefer to relocate, and let the respondent manage their own neighbours.

The Lesson

Privacy is the respondent's right to manage, not yours to enforce on their behalf. The correct move is to offer the respondent a private space or give them the language to ask for privacy themselves — not to create an awkward scene on their behalf. Respecting the community means respecting their social dynamics, not overriding them.

4
Coercion & Prompting — Shyam Pressures for Answers
▼
The Incident

Shyam believed that incomplete surveys were useless and pressured respondents to answer every question. When respondents hesitated, he would prompt them with available answer options — pointing toward specific choices or re-reading options with different emphasis to hint at what he thought was the right answer.

Double Violation

First: pressuring respondents to answer violated voluntary participation principles. Second: prompting with answer options is a data quality violation — it introduces surveyor bias and contaminates the response. Both violations erode the scientific integrity of the study simultaneously.

The Lesson

Incomplete surveys are not useless — coerced or prompted surveys are. A missing response is honest data about respondent discomfort or knowledge limits. A coerced or prompted response is false data that cannot be undone. Respect participant autonomy: if they do not want to answer, record the refusal and move on.

5
J-PAL SA Scenario: The NGO Staff Member Who "Just Wants to Help"
▼
The Situation

You are conducting a baseline survey for a livelihood programme. The local implementing NGO has assigned its own field volunteer to accompany you for "navigation help." When you arrive at a household, the NGO volunteer sits in on the interview. The respondent seems to know them from the programme and is visibly deferential.

The Problem

The NGO volunteer's presence changes what the respondent says. They may underreport problems with the programme to appear grateful. They may answer what they think the NGO wants to hear. Even if the volunteer says nothing, their presence in the room constitutes a social pressure that compromises voluntary participation and data quality simultaneously.

What You Must Do

Before beginning any interview, politely but firmly ask the NGO staff member to wait outside — framing it as standard research procedure, not personal. "Our protocol requires that the interview be conducted privately. Thank you for helping with navigation — I will meet you outside after the interview." If the NGO staff resists or insists on staying, do not begin the interview. Report the situation to your supervisor immediately.

6
J-PAL SA Scenario: The Respondent Who Gives a "Wrong" Answer
▼
The Situation

During an agriculture baseline, a respondent says they cultivated 25 acres this season. Your previous respondents in the same village all reported 2–5 acres. You are certain 25 acres is wrong for a smallholder farmer in this geography. You are also behind on targets and worried this will trigger a supervisor query.

The Temptation

Changing the response to avoid a back-check feels like a small, harmless fix. It is not. This is data falsification — one of the three termination offences. The respondent may own multiple plots, lease land, or be reporting in different units (dismil vs. acre). Outliers are legitimate data points — the quality review process exists precisely to investigate them.

What You Must Do

Record what the respondent said. Use approved probing questions from your field manual (e.g., "Is that in acres or in dismil?") to check for unit confusion. Flag the response in your field notes. Submit accurately and report the outlier to your supervisor at check-in. The decision about how to treat the data belongs to the research team — not to you in the field.

Institutional Oversight

The IRB: Why It Exists and What It Means for You

The Institutional Review Board (IRB) is the independent body that reviews and approves all human subjects research before it begins. You will not interact with it directly — but every protocol you follow, every consent form you use, and every deviation you must report traces back to IRB approval.

What the IRB Does

The IRB reviews research proposals to ensure they comply with applicable regulations, meet ethical standards, and adequately protect participants. It operates on the three Belmont principles: respect, beneficence, and justice.

IRB review levels range from exempt (low-risk studies) to expedited review (minimal risk) to full board review (significant risk, vulnerable populations, complex ethical questions).

When IRB Notification is Required

  • Any changes or additions to the survey or consent form
  • Changes in data security management
  • New study team members
  • Adverse events — problems in the field
  • Any deviation from the approved protocol
  • A potential harm to a participant
  • Annual renewal of the study approval
  • Closing the study protocol
🔑 What This Means for Field Staff

You will not file IRB reports yourself. But you are responsible for reporting field problems to your supervisor so they can. If a respondent is harmed, distressed, or experiences a negative event related to the survey, your supervisor must be informed immediately. If you observe a protocol violation — by yourself or a colleague — it must be reported. Reporting is not disloyalty. It is an IRB requirement.
