Research Ethics & Informed Consent

The moral backbone of fieldwork — why consent exists, how to deliver it, and what happens when it fails
📍 Ethics ModuleVersion 1.0 · 2026⏱ 25 min read
The Foundation

The Four Pillars of Ethical Research

Research ethics is not an abstract academic concept. It is a set of concrete, non-negotiable rules that govern your behavior every minute you are in the field.

🔑 Key Principle

Everything you do in the field — every question you ask, every household you enter, every piece of data you record — is permitted only because a human being has given you their voluntary, informed agreement. Without consent, there is no survey. Without ethics, there is no research.

🛡️ Pillar 1: Do No Harm

Your presence must never make a respondent's situation worse — emotionally, socially, physically, or economically. Before every sensitive question, evaluate: is this person safe to answer honestly?

🤝 Pillar 2: Respect for Persons

Every respondent is an autonomous human being who has the right to decide whether to participate. You never make that decision for them. Respect also extends to those with diminished autonomy.

⚖️ Pillar 3: Beneficence

Maximize possible benefits and minimize possible harms. If you observe a situation that puts a respondent at risk, you have an obligation to act within your role and refer through proper channels.

🏛️ Pillar 4: Justice (Fairness)

The burdens and benefits of research must be distributed fairly. You do not over-survey easy households while skipping hard-to-reach ones. You treat every respondent identically.

⚠️ What Harm Looks Like in Practice
  • A woman answers questions about her husband's drinking in front of in-laws — the family punishes her later. Cause: surveyor's failure to ensure privacy.
  • A respondent discloses domestic violence; the surveyor says "Go to the police." The husband finds out; violence escalates. Cause: unsolicited advice the surveyor was not qualified to give.
Compliance

The Ethics Committee (IRB)

Every legitimate study must be reviewed and approved by an Institutional Review Board (IRB) before any fieldwork begins.

Rule Why
You cannot modify the consent processThe consent script was approved exactly as written. You may not add, subtract, or paraphrase it in ways that change its meaning.
You cannot add questionsUnapproved questions have not been reviewed for ethical risk.
You cannot skip consentEven if the respondent says "Just get on with it." It is a legal and ethical requirement.
Violations shut down studiesMonths or years of work are lost. Responsible individuals face immediate termination.
Execution

The Informed Consent Process

Informed consent is not a form you get signed. It is a process through which a respondent reaches a genuine, uncoerced decision.

✅ Model Introduction

"Namaste. My name is [your name]. I am a surveyor working with [research organization]. We are conducting a research study in this area to understand [one-sentence topic]. I would like to explain the study to you and, if you are willing, ask if you would be interested in participating. May I take a few minutes of your time to explain?"

The Seven Essential Elements

1

Purpose of the Study

What the study is about and why it is being conducted. Use a one-sentence summary.

2

What Participation Involves

What the respondent will do, for how long. Be honest about duration.

3

Voluntary Participation

Participation is entirely their choice. No penalty. Access to government services is not affected.

4

Risks & Discomfort

Some questions may be personal. They may skip any question without consequence.

5

Confidentiality

Everything they say is strictly confidential. Names will not appear in reports.

6

No Direct Benefit

Participation does not guarantee money, services, or program enrolment.

7

Contact Information

Provide the supervisor's number and the research organization helpline.

✅ What Good Consent Looks Like

The surveyor explains the topic in plain language. She reads the full consent form at a natural pace, pausing after each section. When the respondent asks about pensions, she clarifies the survey has nothing to do with government services. Time: 6 minutes.

❌ What a Red-Flag Consent Looks Like

The surveyor arrives and says: "The sarpanch sent me. I need to ask you some questions for a government survey." He hands the form and says "Just sign here." The respondent signs without explanation. Time: 30 seconds. This interview is ethically invalid.

Complex Situations

Special Consent Scenarios

Standard consent assumes a literate adult in a private setting making a free choice. In fieldwork, this ideal is often the exception.

Consent for Minors (Under 18)

Two separate consents are required: Parental/Guardian Consent (all elements communicated to the parent) and Child Assent (the minor agrees using age-appropriate language). If either refuses, you do not proceed.

Consent When Literacy Is Limited

  • Read the entire consent form aloud, in the respondent's language, at a pace they can follow.
  • After each major section, pause and ask: "Does this make sense?"
  • Use thumbprint with a witness — do not ask them to sign what they cannot read.

Third-Party Interference

SituationYour Response
Village leader says "I give you permission to survey everyone"Thank the leader. Explain that you still need individual consent from each respondent.
Husband answers "Yes" on wife's behalfThank him. Then directly ask the wife whether she is comfortable participating.
Someone demands to see your dataDecline politely. Offer supervisor contact details. Never hand over your device.
Protocols

Handling Refusals & Withdrawals

SituationWhat You Do
Respondent refuses before survey beginsThank them for their time. Do not argue. Record the refusal. Move on.
Respondent consents to some modules but refuses othersProceed with consented modules. Skip refused ones. Document clearly.
Respondent wants to stop mid-interviewStop immediately. Save partial data. Offer option to continue another time.
Respondent verbally consents but refuses to sign/thumbprintCheck protocol. If protocol requires signature and respondent refuses, you cannot proceed.
⚠️ Never Do This
  • Never say "The sarpanch has approved this" to pressure someone.
  • Never imply that refusal will affect access to government schemes.
  • Never circle back to a household that has refused, hoping for a different answer.
