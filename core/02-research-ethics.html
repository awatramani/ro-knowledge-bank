Research Ethics & Informed Consent

The moral backbone of fieldwork — why consent exists, how to deliver it, and what happens when it fails
📘 Foundation Module · Chapter A2 Version 1.0 · 2026 ⏱ 25 min read
🔑 Key Principle

Everything you do in the field — every question you ask, every household you enter, every piece of data you record — is permitted only because a human being has given you their voluntary, informed agreement. Without consent, there is no survey. Without ethics, there is no research.

A2.1

The Four Pillars of Ethical Research

Research ethics is not an abstract academic concept. It is a set of concrete, non-negotiable rules that govern your behaviour every minute you are in the field. These rules exist because of a painful history — researchers in many countries conducted studies that harmed participants physically, psychologically, or by violating their dignity. The ethical frameworks used today were developed specifically to prevent those harms from recurring.

🛡️ Pillar 1: Do No Harm

Your presence must never make a respondent's situation worse — emotionally, socially, physically, or economically. Before every sensitive question, evaluate: is this person safe to answer honestly? If not, adjust the environment, defer the module, or skip and document.

🤝 Pillar 2: Respect for Persons

Every respondent is an autonomous human being who has the right to decide whether to participate. You never make that decision for them. Respect also extends to those with diminished autonomy — the elderly, children, those under social pressure — who require additional protections.

⚖️ Pillar 3: Beneficence

Maximise possible benefits and minimise possible harms. You are not a passive instrument — if you observe a situation that puts a respondent at risk (e.g. a medical emergency, signs of abuse), you have an obligation to act within your role and refer through proper channels.

🏛️ Pillar 4: Justice (Fairness)

The burdens and benefits of research must be distributed fairly. You do not over-survey easy households while skipping hard-to-reach ones. You treat every respondent identically regardless of caste, religion, gender, or economic status. Every household in your sample deserves equal effort.

⚠️ What Harm Looks Like in Practice
  • A woman answers questions about her husband's drinking in front of in-laws — the family punishes her later. Cause: surveyor's failure to ensure privacy.
  • Neighbours see a farmer being interviewed and assume he is receiving benefits. Social friction increases. Cause: surveyor being conspicuous.
  • A respondent discloses domestic violence; the surveyor says "Go to the police." The husband finds out; violence escalates. Cause: unsolicited advice the surveyor was not qualified to give.
  • An adolescent girl is asked about menstrual hygiene while her father and brothers are present. Cause: surveyor's failure to arrange a private setting.
A2.2

The Ethics Committee (IRB)

Every legitimate research study involving human subjects must be reviewed and approved by an Institutional Review Board (IRB) or Ethics Committee before any fieldwork begins. This body examines the study design, questionnaire, consent process, and data protection plan to ensure the study will not harm participants.

What the IRB Means for You

Rule Why
You cannot modify the consent processThe consent script was approved exactly as written. You may not add, subtract, or paraphrase it in ways that change its meaning.
You cannot add questions to the surveyUnapproved questions have not been reviewed for ethical risk. If you think a question is missing, tell your supervisor.
You cannot skip the consent processEven if the respondent says "Just get on with it." It is a legal and ethical requirement — not optional.
You cannot share data with anyone outside the research teamThis includes government officials, NGO workers, village leaders, family members, and other respondents.
Violations can shut down the entire studyMonths or years of work — and the evidence it would have generated — are lost. Responsible individuals face immediate termination and may face legal consequences.
A2.3

The Informed Consent Process

Informed consent is not a form you get signed. It is a process through which a respondent reaches a genuine, uncoerced decision to participate after understanding what it involves. The form documents the process. The conversation, explanation, questions answered, and voluntary agreement — that is what matters.

Stage 1: Before Consent — Approach & Environment

Consent begins before you open your mouth. Choose a space where the respondent can think and decide without social pressure — not in a group of neighbours, not at the doorstep while cooking, and not in the presence of a village leader who brought you there.

✅ Model Introduction

"Namaste. My name is [your name]. I am a surveyor working with [research organization]. We are conducting a research study in this area to understand [one-sentence topic]. I would like to explain the study to you and, if you are willing, ask if you would be interested in participating. May I take a few minutes of your time to explain?"

Stage 2: The Seven Essential Elements

You must communicate all seven of these elements to the respondent. The meaning of each must be clearly conveyed and understood.

1

Purpose of the Study

What the study is about and why it is being conducted. Use a one-sentence summary. Avoid jargon.

2

What Participation Involves

What the respondent will do: answer questions, for how long, on what topics. Be honest about duration — if 45 minutes, say 45 minutes.

3

Voluntary Participation

Participation is entirely their choice. They can say no now or stop at any time. No penalty. Access to government services or benefits will not be affected.

4

Risks & Discomfort

Some questions may be personal. They may skip any question without consequence.

5

Confidentiality

Everything they say is strictly confidential. Names will not appear in reports. Answers are combined with thousands of others and analysed as a group.

6

No Direct Benefit

Participation does not guarantee money, services, or program enrolment. The research may improve programmes in the future, but no immediate personal benefit.

7

Contact Information

If the respondent has questions or complaints, provide the supervisor's name/number and the research organization helpline.

Good vs. Bad Consent in Practice

✅ What Good Consent Looks Like

The surveyor introduces herself by name, states the organization, and explains the topic in plain language. She reads the full consent form at a natural pace, pausing after each section. When the respondent asks about pensions, she clarifies the survey has nothing to do with government services. The respondent agrees freely. Time: 6 minutes.

❌ What a Red-Flag Consent Looks Like

The surveyor arrives and says: "The sarpanch sent me. I need to ask you some questions for a government survey. It won't take long." He hands the form and says "Just sign here." The respondent, who cannot read, signs without explanation. Time: 30 seconds. This entire interview is ethically invalid.

🔑 The Four Conditions of Valid Consent

All four must be met simultaneously — if any one is missing, the consent is not legally or ethically valid: Information (respondent was told everything), Comprehension (respondent actually understood), Voluntariness (choice was free from coercion or pressure), and Documentation (process was properly recorded).

A2.4

Special Consent Scenarios

Standard consent assumes a literate adult in a private setting making a free choice. In Indian fieldwork, this ideal is more the exception than the rule. These special scenarios require additional protocols.

Consent for Minors (Under 18)

Two separate consents are required: Parental/Guardian Consent (all seven elements communicated to the parent) and Child Assent (the minor agrees using age-appropriate language). If either the parent refuses or the child does not assent, the interview does not proceed.

Consent When Literacy Is Limited

A significant proportion of respondents in rural India may not read the consent form. This does not reduce their right to informed consent — it increases your responsibility.

  • Read the entire consent form aloud, in the respondent's language, at a pace they can follow.
  • After each major section, pause and ask: "Does this make sense? Do you have any questions?"
  • Use thumbprint with a witness, or audio-recorded verbal consent — do not ask them to sign what they cannot read.
  • The witness should be someone the respondent trusts (not you, not your colleague, not the village leader).

Third-Party Interference

Situation Your Response
Village leader says "I give you permission to survey everyone"Thank the leader. Explain that you still need individual consent from each respondent as required by the ethics committee.
Husband answers "Yes" on wife's behalfThank him. Then directly ask the wife whether she is comfortable participating. Her answer is the only one that counts.
Family member pressures respondent to answer while they watchPolitely request privacy. If impossible, proceed only with modules the respondent is comfortable answering in that setting.
Someone demands to see your dataDecline politely. Offer supervisor contact details. Never hand over your device. Report the incident.
A2.5

Handling Refusals & Withdrawals

Situation What You Do
Respondent refuses before the survey beginsThank them for their time. Do not argue, persuade, or show disappointment. Record the refusal. Move on.
Respondent consents to some modules but refuses othersThis is partial consent. Proceed with consented modules. Skip refused ones. Document clearly which modules were refused and why.
Respondent wants to stop mid-interviewStop immediately. Save partial data. Thank them. Offer the option to continue another time if they wish. Do not pressure.
You return for a second visit to the same householdIf the second visit is a new survey round (midline, endline), a full new consent process is required.
Respondent verbally consents but refuses to sign or thumbprintCheck your study protocol. Many studies allow audio-recorded verbal consent. If protocol requires signature and respondent refuses, you cannot proceed even with verbal agreement. Record and report.
⚠️ Never Do This
  • Never say "The sarpanch/pradhan has approved this" to pressure someone into participating.
  • Never imply that refusal will affect their access to government schemes or benefits.
  • Never get a family member to convince a reluctant respondent on your behalf.
  • Never circle back to a household that has refused, hoping for a different answer. One refusal is final.

🔑 Key Principle

A respondent's trust is the most valuable resource in field research. It is more valuable than your device, your survey instrument, or your daily target. Without trust, you get polite lies instead of honest answers. The chain begins with you and the three minutes you spend on consent. Make those three minutes count.
