The Academy

Training is Not an Event. It is a System.

A poorly trained team produces data that looks clean but is systematically wrong. The Training Academy exists to make that outcome impossible — through rigorous preparation, certification gates, and continuous calibration at every stage of the deployment lifecycle.

Training Lifecycle

Three Phases. No Shortcuts.

Every project training must move through all three phases. Compressing or skipping any phase transfers cost from the preparation stage — where it is cheap to fix — to data collection, where it is catastrophically expensive.

Phase 1

Manual Drafting

Begin T-6 weeks. Joint RA + SC ownership. Mandatory Director (RO) & Director (Research) sign-off before distribution.

Phase 2

Training Delivery

6-7 days minimum. Questionnaire walkthrough, role-plays, daily quizzes, device practice. Field pilot day mandatory.

Phase 3

Certification & Go/No-Go

PDRA pass required. All 8 role-plays signed off. SC has authority to block launch if gates are not met.

People and Culture

Values, Code of Conduct, and Organisational Culture in the Field

Who We Are • How We Work • How We Care for Each Other and the Communities We Serve
Respect | Care | Rigour | Responsibility & Accountability | Integrity

For All Field Staff (Surveyors, Field Supervisors, SCs, RAs) • Version 1.0

Context

Stated Assumptions

This document applies to all field staff working on J-PAL South Asia research projects, regardless of role, contract type, or duration.

Universality

The five organisational values and Field Code of Conduct apply to ALL staff. There are no exemptions based on seniority.

Three Arenas of Conduct

Behaviour is assessed in three arenas: (a) within communities, (b) within the team, and (c) in relation to policies.

Field Specificity

Values are not abstract principles. 'Rigour' is what you do with a vague respondent answer at 4:30 PM when you are tired.

The Big Picture

The Research Value Chain

J-PAL South Asia works to reduce poverty through rigorous policy evaluation. The integrity of this methodology depends entirely on measurement quality. If measurement is flawed, the policy recommendation can do harm.

Stage Who Does It What Field Staff Must Know
1. Study Design PI / Research Team The instrument reflects deliberate decisions. Do not improvise.
2. IRB Approval Ethics Board Consent and data protection are legal/ethical obligations.
3. Data Collection Field Teams (YOU) The only step that cannot be corrected later. A wrong measurement is simply wrong.
4. QA & Analysis SC + RA + Analysts QA process is not a punishment — it is a safety net.
Why Culture Matters The Team That Had Two Cultures (MP, 2022)

A team had two wings. Wing A's supervisor created psychological safety (<3% discrepancy). Wing B's supervisor ruled by fear, punishing slow progress. The Result: Wing B fabricated 22 forms for a flooded village because honest non-contact reports were punished.

Learning: A Supervisor who creates a safe space for honest reporting produces better data than any monitoring system.

The Core Framework

The Five Values in Action

Values are specific operational choices made daily under difficult field conditions. Explore how they apply in practice.

1. Respect
2. Care
3. Rigour
4. Accountability
5. Integrity

Respect: The First & Most Visible Value

Visible within 30 seconds. It requires recognition of dignity, time, and autonomy across the community, the team, and respondents.

🚩 The Permission Myth

Saying "This is a government survey" or "Participation is mandatory" is a disrespectful lie that exploits authority. J-PAL surveys are VOLUNTARY. Deception destroys future trust.

Arena What Good Looks Like What Disrespect Looks Like
Community Seek leader acknowledgment first. Use local dialect greetings. Accept water politely. Photographing without consent. Arriving in overly casual attire.
Team Supervisors address errors privately. SCs consult before reassigning. Public shaming (e.g., morning briefings). Complaining to peers.
Respondents Silent patience when recalling. Genuine thanks. Validating their choice to stop. Sighing/checking time. Typing while they speak without looking at them.

Care: Operational Intelligence

Care means noticing when a colleague or respondent is not okay. Exhausted or frightened staff cannot produce high-quality work.

Team Care Must-Dos

  • Daily Pulse: 'Sab theek hai?' every morning.
  • Buddy Culture: No one works a remote location alone.
  • Illness Protocol: Sick staff do not work. Targets do not override health.

🚩 Boundaries of Care

Never promise a respondent:

  • "I'll take this to the government."
  • "I will help you." (Unless referral exists).
  • "You will get benefits after this survey."
Wellbeing Concern Field Response Protocol
Distress / Crying Stop. Set tablet down. Ask to pause. Inform Supervisor.
Domestic Violence Acknowledge bravery. Offer connection to help. Escalate to SC. Do NOT give personal number.

Rigour: Precision as a Practice

Rigour is the choice to probe when it's 4:30 PM, you are tired, and an answer is vague.

The Comments Field

The 'Field Notes' box is your professional voice. An empty comments field for a complex interview (proxy, interrupted) is a red flag. Note ambiguities to preserve context.

The Rigour Test Low Rigour High Rigour
Option doesn't fit exactly Select closest option. Move on. Select 'Other', type description, flag to Supervisor.
"I don't remember" Record 'DK' and move on. Probe with event anchoring. Record rough range in 'Other'.
Household locked (3rd visit) Record 'refused' (easier). Record 'Non-contact - 3 attempts' with dates/times.

Responsibility & Accountability

An accountable person says: "This was my mistake, here is what happened and how we fix it."

Situation Unaccountable Response Accountable Response
Skipped 2 questions Submit form. Hope QA misses it. Flag to Supervisor same day with exact missed questions.
Couldn't reach 3 HHs Mark as 'refused' to hit targets. Mark as 'non-contact'. Plan revisit with Supervisor.
Wrong HH address surveyed Submit anyway (same village). Do NOT submit. Delete form. Record in Supervisor report.
Learning vs. Blame Cultures

In a learning culture, errors are expected, disclosed, and used to improve systems. A blame culture produces hidden errors. Hidden errors compound.

Integrity & Excellence

The choice you make when the outcome looks the same whether done well or poorly — and the poor way is easier.

Direction What It Looks Like
When No One Is Watching Same interview quality whether or not the Supervisor accompanies you.
Upward Honesty Reporting problems to the SC immediately, not waiting for automated QA to flag them.
Downward Honesty Giving honest, constructive feedback to junior staff instead of protecting them from reality.
Lateral Honesty Acknowledging when a colleague finds an error. Not blaming peers for shared failures.
Non-Negotiables

Protection Policies

Legal and ethical obligations in all field sites, vehicles, and accommodations.

🛑 POSH (Prevention of Sexual Harassment)

Applies to physical, verbal, visual, and digital (WhatsApp/messages) conduct. The test is whether the conduct was unwelcome, not the intent.

Reporting Flow:

Supervisor ➔ Survey Coordinator ➔ RA / POSH IC
  • • You may report anonymously.
  • • Zero Retaliation: Strict protection for complainants.
  • • Silence is Complicity: Supervisors MUST transmit complaints within 24h. SCs must transmit to IC within 7 days. Do not mediate informally.

🛡️ Child Protection

No child is harmed as a result of our work.

  • • Consent: Never interview without guardian.
  • • Isolation: Never be alone with a child.
  • • Disclosure: STOP probing. LISTEN. RECORD. REPORT to SC same day.
Zero Tolerance: Social Media Never post images of children to WhatsApp status, IG, or X. Field sites are not tourism. Violation results in immediate removal.
Code of Conduct

The Three Arenas & Ethical Dilemmas

Excellence in one arena does not compensate for failures in another.

1. Community

  • Honest introductions.
  • Observe local norms.
  • Zero substance/alcohol use on field days.
  • No personal/romantic community relationships.

2. Team

  • Resolve disagreements professionally.
  • No hierarchy abuse or cliques.
  • No financial transactions between staff.
  • Disclose conflicts of interest.

3. Organisation

  • Permanent data confidentiality.
  • Appropriate resource use (tablets).
  • Strict financial integrity on expenses.

Ethical Dilemmas in Practice

Dilemma 1: Distressed Respondent

A widow cries discussing housing rejection but says "keep asking."

Resolution: Rigour needs accuracy. Distressed recall is unreliable. Pause the survey.

Dilemma 2: Supervisor is Wrong

Supervisor says to substitute households if a module is refused.

Resolution: Protocol overrides authority. Respectfully escalate to SC privately. Do not defy publicly.

Dilemma 3: Hospitality Trap

Sarpanch invites team to a meal on Day 1.

Resolution: Decline politely to avoid sampling bias. Offer to share a meal at the END of deployment.

Dilemma 4: Colleague in Trouble

A close friend on the team is fabricating survey durations.

Resolution: Speak privately, urge self-reporting. If they refuse, report them. Silence is complicity.

Chapter 12

Leadership Presence & Team Rhythm

Data collection is teamwork. Leadership presence connects people to values. It means being in the field, curious, and building systems for rapid upward information flow.

The 30-40% Rule

RAs must spend 30-40% of active data collection days in the field. Direct observation catches what remote reports miss.

The Weekly Loop

Every Friday: Track deviation between Supervisor tracking sheets and SurveyCTO data. This surfaces reality.

Skip-Level Chats

Informal talks bypassing hierarchy to hear unfiltered voices. Rule: Absolute confidentiality.

  • Wellbeing: 'Kaise ho genuinely?'
  • Field conditions: 'Sabse badi pareshani kya thi?'
  • Recognition: 'Aapne sabse accha kya kiya?'

Specific Recognition

"Great job team" is white noise. Keep "Running Notes" of specific actions. "Sunita, you caught your Supervisor's ID error and reported it. That saved 9 forms." Recognition at the right moment changes behaviour.

The Training Manual

The definitive blueprint for field excellence. A robust training manual is the single most critical document standing between a theoretical research design and high-quality, ground-level data collection.

A Training Manual is not merely a printed copy of the questionnaire. It is a comprehensive instructional guide that translates complex academic intent into actionable, localized field protocols. It empowers surveyors to navigate ambiguity, build trust, probe neutrally, and capture reality without bias.

1. Ownership, Timeline & Approvals

The creation of the Training Manual is a highly collaborative, rigorous process. It cannot be rushed in the week before training begins.

Joint Development (RA & SC)

The manual is a joint resource developed continuously by the Research Associate (RA) and the Survey Coordinator (SC). The RA provides the scientific intent, ethical guidelines, and survey logic. The SC translates this into field reality, local vernacular, contextual probing strategies, and logistical workflows.

The Timeline Trigger

Drafting the Training Manual must begin as soon as the hiring request is raised. By the time candidates are being interviewed, the core modules of the manual should already be in the drafting phase to ensure the training architecture matches the skills being hired for.

Mandatory Approval Gate Before any manual is sent to print or shared with incoming field staff, the final draft MUST be submitted to both the Director (Research Operations) and the Director (Research) for comprehensive review and final approval.
Development Lifecycle
  • T-Minus 6 Weeks: Hiring Request Raised. RA & SC begin drafting core modules and definitions.
  • T-Minus 4 Weeks: Translations tested. Probing guidelines added based on early pre-pilots.
  • T-Minus 2 Weeks: Agenda finalized. Logistics and tablet management protocols integrated.
  • T-Minus 1 Week: Submitted to Director (RO) and Director (Research) for final sign-off.

2. Structuring the Training Agenda

A well-structured manual includes a clear, minute-by-minute training agenda. A standard 6-to-7 day training must balance theoretical knowledge with aggressive, repetitive practical application.

The agenda must explicitly detail:

  • Resource Persons: Clearly identifying who leads which session (e.g., PI for Study Overview, SC for Field Logistics, RA for Data Quality).
  • General Sessions: Dedicated hours for J-PAL protocols, ethical conduct, device management, and gender/safeguarding policies.
  • Questionnaire Walkthrough: Screen-by-screen reading, explaining the intent of every single module.
  • Classroom Practice (Pairs): Supervised role-play. One acts as the respondent, one as the surveyor. SCs and RAs must actively monitor and correct probing techniques.
  • Field Practice: Mandatory full-day deployment in a non-sample village to stress-test skills in real-world conditions.
  • Tests & Mock Quizzes: Daily written/digital quizzes to test retention, culminating in a final certification mock interview before field deployment.

3. Core Attributes of Surveying

Before touching the specific project questionnaire, the manual must establish the universal attributes of a high-quality surveyor. The SC and RA must build comprehensive sections detailing how to operate in the field.

Building Trust & Informed Consent

Trust is the currency of field research. The manual must provide exact scripts and behavioral guidelines for entering a village, introducing the organization, addressing community leaders, and securing true, uncoerced informed consent. It must cover how to explain data privacy in simple, vernacular terms.

Tackling Challenges & Refusals

Surveyors must be trained to differentiate between a "Soft Refusal" (I'm busy right now, come back later) and a "Hard Refusal" (I will not participate, leave my property). The manual must provide de-escalation strategies, methods for handling hostile crowds, and clear escalation protocols to the Field Supervisor.

Neutrality & Positioning

Surveyors must learn to maintain an absolute neutral posture—neither approving nor disapproving of a respondent's answers. The manual must highlight the dangers of leading questions, non-verbal bias (nodding, frowning), and making assumptions based on visual socio-economic indicators.

🗣️ The Golden Rule of Probing

The manual must dedicate significant space to Probing Techniques. If a respondent says "I earn enough," the surveyor cannot write "Sufficient." They must be taught to use Laddering ("Could you help me understand what enough means in rupees per month?") and Strategic Silence (waiting 3 seconds after an incomplete answer to encourage the respondent to elaborate without leading them).

4. The Questionnaire Deep Dive

This is the heart of the manual. The RA and SC must break down the questionnaire not just by section, but question by question. A raw survey form tells the surveyor what to ask. The manual must tell them why they are asking it, and how to handle it when things go wrong.

For every critical or complex module, the manual must define:

  • The Intent: Why is the researcher asking this? (e.g., "We ask about fertilizer usage not to judge their farming, but to calculate input costs.")
  • Definitions: Clear, localized definitions of complex terms. (e.g., What counts as a 'pucca' house? What defines a 'joint family' in this specific district?)
  • Tricky Scenarios: What to do if the respondent doesn't know the answer, refuses to answer, or gives an answer that contradicts a previous module.
  • Observation vs. Asking: Clear instructions on which questions must be explicitly asked, and which data points (like roof material) should be captured via silent observation.
Exemplar Layout: Module Breakdown
Module 3: Agricultural Income
Q3.4: "In the last Kharif season, how much did you spend on labor?"
Intent: To capture out-of-pocket expenditure on hired help. Do NOT include family labor.

Definitions: 'Kharif season' refers to the monsoon crop (typically June-October in this region).

Probing Instruction: Farmers often forget cash paid to daily wage workers during harvest. Ask specifically: "Did you hire anyone for sowing? Did you hire anyone for cutting?" If paid in grains instead of cash, estimate the market value of the grains.

Training Minimum Must-Dos

The Irreducible Minimum Architecture Every Project Training Must Meet

Mandatory Hours • Mandatory Topics • Mandatory Role-Plays • Sign-Off Requirements
Before the First Household Visit, This Checklist Must Be Complete — No Exceptions

Primary Users: SCs, RAs | Reference: Field Supervisors • Version 1.0

Context

Stated Assumptions

This document is primarily addressed to the Survey Coordinator (SC) who designs and delivers field staff training. The RA uses it to audit training quality.

Instrument Calibration

Minimums defined for Short (<25 min, 3 modules) and Standard (25–75 min, 4–8 modules). Complex instruments require additional hours.

The Floor, Not the Target

These are minimum requirements. An SC who is 'done' after meeting the minimum has met the compliance bar — not the excellence bar.

Go/No-Go Standard

No survey launch should proceed if any component is incomplete. The consequences of an undertrained team are worse than a delayed launch.

Chapter 1

Why Minimums Exist

Every SC believes their training was adequate. The ones who discover it wasn't have usually already launched data collection. Every compression of training time transfers the cost from the training phase — where it is cheap to fix — to the data collection phase, where it is expensive.

✅ SC Authority to Block Launch

SCs have the professional obligation to decline launch if minimums aren't met. If pressured to launch, the SC must document the instruction and their recommendation against it.

Cost Analysis The 3-Day Training That Became a 3-Week Remediation (Bihar)

A nutrition baseline was compressed to 1.5 days. Role-plays for household ID and sync protocols were skipped.

  • 34% of forms had GPS >500m off polygon.
  • 12 sync failures = 8 permanently lost forms.
  • 3 complaints from leaders due to skipped entry protocols.

Cost: 3 weeks of remediation. ₹2.8 lakh in extra costs. The skipped training would have cost ₹12,000.

The Quantified Cost of Training Gaps

Training Gap Cost to the Project Catchability?
Household definition misunderstood (chulha rule) ₹12k–20k per 100 HHs revisited. 3–5 day delay. Catchable via back-check (requires re-visits).
Probing not practiced (vague answers accepted) Key recall variables systematically understated by 20–35%. Partially catchable — bias is hard to correct post-data.
Consent script not practiced IRB violation. Extreme cases: data destruction & restart. Not catchable after the fact — IRB is binary.
Data sync protocol not practiced Lost forms: ₹500–800 revisit cost + analyst time per form. Not recoverable if device is lost.

Ongoing & Post-Survey

Treating launch as the end of training is a common error.

During Deployment
  • Error found in back-check: Supervisor runs 20-min corrective session within 24h.
  • New joiner: Full 3-day training + Pilot required. No shortcuts.
  • Form update pushed: SC runs 30-min tablet update practice before next field day.

Post-Survey Debrief (2 Hours)

Capture intelligence before the team disperses. Ask:

  • What were the 3 most confusing questions?
  • What situation did we encounter that the manual missed?
  • What would you add to Day 1 next time?
Chapter 4

Time Budget & Pilot Day

Content delivered over 3 days produces 3-5x better retention than 1.5 days.

⚠️ 2-Day Accelerated Format

Permissible ONLY if BOTH conditions are TRUE simultaneously:

  • ✅ Instrument is <25 min AND ≤3 modules.
  • ✅ ≥50% of surveyor team are returning staff trained in the past 12 months.
The Buffer Day Is Not Optional

Build a half-day buffer before Pilot Day for overnight retention and SC quiz review.

🚀 The Pilot Day

The last opportunity to course-correct. Real households, real data (not included in study).

Target: 2 forms per surveyor by 5 PM. Quality > Volume.
Supervision: Accompany ≥2 surveyors for full interviews.
SC Review: Check duration, GPS, mandatory fields by 6 PM.
