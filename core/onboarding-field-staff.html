1.1 · Who We Are

J-PAL — A Global Network Committed to Ending Poverty

J-PAL (the Abdul Latif Jameel Poverty Action Lab) is a global research centre whose mission is to reduce poverty by ensuring that policy is informed by scientific evidence. Founded at MIT in 2003, J-PAL now has a global network of more than 1,000 affiliated researchers across 7 regional offices — covering Africa, Europe, Latin America & the Caribbean, Middle East & North Africa, North America, South Asia, and Southeast Asia.

J-PAL's primary method is the Randomised Controlled Trial (RCT) — a scientifically rigorous method for testing whether a social programme actually works. A randomly selected group receives the intervention; another does not. By comparing outcomes between these groups — through surveys conducted by people like you — researchers can determine with confidence whether the programme made a difference.

Why J-PAL Exists

For decades, governments and donors invested billions in social programmes that sounded good but didn't actually work — because nobody rigorously tested them. J-PAL exists to change that: to replace guesswork with evidence, and to ensure that when governments spend public money on programmes for poor communities, those programmes actually help. The data you collect is what makes that evidence.

1.3 · Where You Fit

The Research Value Chain — Your Role at Its Centre

Understanding where your work sits in the larger chain of research helps you understand the stakes of what you do. The chain starts with a researcher asking a policy-relevant question. It ends with a government or donor making a decision about a programme that affects real people. Field data collection is the only step in that chain that cannot be corrected after the fact.

StageWho Does ItWhat Field Staff Must Know
1. Study DesignPrincipal Investigator (PI)The PI has defined exactly what question to answer and how. The survey instrument reflects deliberate measurement decisions. Do not improvise.
2. IRB ApprovalResearch Team + Ethics BoardAn independent ethics board reviewed and approved this study. The consent process you deliver is a legal and ethical obligation, not a formality.
3. Instrument DesignResearch Team + RA + SCEvery question has a reason. If a question seems redundant or uncomfortable, ask your SC — don't skip it.
4. Field Data CollectionSupervisors + Surveyors (YOU)This is the only step that cannot be corrected after the fact. A wrong measurement is simply wrong. A fabricated survey is worse than a missing one.
5. Data Quality AssuranceSC + RA + Analysis TeamBack-checks, GPS audits, and duration checks happen because field data always has errors. QC is not a punishment — it is a safety net that protects everyone.
6. Analysis & PublicationResearch TeamYour data becomes tables, regressions, and eventually a published paper. Your name will not appear on it. But the quality of your work is embedded in every number.
7. Policy EngagementJ-PAL + Research PartnersGovernments and donors use the findings to make decisions about programmes. Your data influences those decisions — about nutrition, education, livelihoods, and health for communities like the ones you visit.

⚡ The Cost of Bad Field Data

A wrong measurement cannot be "fixed" in analysis — it is simply wrong. When a surveyor fabricates a form, they aren't saving time; they are inserting a lie into research that governments will use to decide whether to expand or cut a programme serving millions. The chain from your form to their lives is shorter than you think.

1.4 · Our Five Values

Respect · Care · Rigour · Responsibility & Accountability · Integrity

These five values apply to everyone — permanent staff, contractual staff, field staff, and office staff. They are not aspirations on a wall. They are the operational standard against which your conduct is measured, every day, in the field. Each value is defined here not as a principle but as a set of specific behaviours visible in field settings.

Respect · Izzat

Izzat Dena — इज़्ज़त देना

Respect is not manners. Manners are what you do at a stranger's door. Respect is what you do when the stranger is from a different caste, when the respondent is older and gives a confusing answer, when the household is very poor and the smell is uncomfortable. Respect in those moments — not polite moments — is the value.

The Field Test: "Am I treating this respondent with the same care and patience I would give to a respondent in a wealthier household? Am I addressing my junior colleague privately when I have feedback, not in front of the group?"

Care · Parwah

Parwah Karna — परवाह करना

Care means noticing — when your colleague is quiet for the third evening in a row, when a respondent's hands tremble while describing last year's crop failure, when you yourself are running on empty and haven't eaten since morning. Care is the antidote to treating people as instruments of a task.

The Field Test: "Have I checked in with my colleague today — not about survey count, but about how they are? Have I noticed when a respondent is distressed and paused? Have I eaten and hydrated properly today?"

Rigour · Kadi Mehnat

Sach mein Koshish — सच्ची कोशिश

Rigour is the most demanding value. The test is not whether you probe well on Day 1 when you are fresh and motivated. The test is whether you probe well at 4:30 PM in Week 3 when you are tired, behind schedule, and the answer is "probably fine anyway."

The 4:30 PM Test: "The respondent says 'Kharch kuch 3-4 hazaar hoga.' Do I record 3500 and move on (5 seconds) or probe by sub-category to get a real figure (2 more minutes)? Rigour means choosing the harder option every time."

Responsibility & Accountability · Zimmedaari

Zimmedaari Lena — ज़िम्मेदारी लेना

Responsibility is the commitment you make when you take the assignment. Accountability is what you do when the assignment produces a result you didn't want. The most expensive words in field operations are: "Mujhe pata tha par maine bola nahi." (I knew but I didn't say anything.)

The Field Test: "If I completed a form in 22 minutes on a 35-minute instrument — do I explain it away, or do I look at my own practice and tell my Supervisor honestly what happened?"

Integrity · Imaandaari

Imaandaari — ईमानदारी

Integrity is what you do when no one is watching at all: when the GPS isn't capturing and you could type any coordinate; when the form duration is short and you could explain it away; when you know a colleague is doing something wrong and no one has noticed yet.

The Invisible Observer: "Imagine a person whose only job is to watch you — every interview, every form, every moment in the field. They see everything. At the end of the project, they write one paragraph describing you as a field worker. What does that paragraph say?"
1.5 · Values Under Pressure

How Values Get Tested in the Field — Real Scenarios

Values are easy to hold in comfortable conditions. What matters is whether you hold them when it's difficult. These scenarios represent real situations where the five values are tested — and what living each value looks like in that moment.

🗣️ Scenario · Respect — Prayagraj, Module 6

The Situation: Surveyor Aman was administering Module 6 to Savitri Devi (42) when he noticed her name on the roster. Without asking, he began calling her "Savitri ji." Halfway through the module, Savitri paused: "Mujhe Savitri mat bolo — ghar mein naam nahi lete." (Don't call me Savitri — at home we don't use the name.)

In many rural communities, a married woman's personal name is not used by outsiders. Before Module 6, the correct question is: "Aapko main kya bolun — didi, bhabhi, ya kuch aur?" (What should I call you — didi, bhabhi, or something else?)

📌 Value in Action: Respect means asking how someone wants to be addressed — not assuming. It takes 5 seconds and communicates the deepest kind of respect: that you see the person in front of you, not just a respondent with a code.
🗣️ Scenario · Rigour — Katihar, Bihar · 4:30 PM Test

The Situation: Surveyor Deepa is at her last household of the day. It's hot, she's tired, and she's behind her target by 1 survey. The respondent mentions their agricultural income as "roughly 15,000 for the year." Module 3 requires itemised recall by crop.

Choice A: Record 15,000 and submit. Done in 3 minutes. Choice B: "Thoda aur yaad karne ki koshish karein — pehle gehu batayein, phir dhan?" (Let's try to remember a bit more — tell me wheat first, then paddy?) Takes 8 more minutes.

Across 600 households, the difference between Choice A and Choice B on agricultural income is an average of ₹3,200 per household — ₹19.2 lakh in total measurement error on a single variable.

📌 Value in Action: Rigour means making the right choice at 4:30 PM on a hard day. Not because the QA system will catch the rounded number — it might not. But because the respondent's actual income matters to the analysis, and you chose to do the work to get it right.
🗣️ Scenario · Integrity — Uttarakhand, Week 4

The Situation: Surveyors Kamlesh and Priya were assigned to the same village cluster in Week 4. On Day 23, Kamlesh's tablet battery died at 3 PM with 2 forms left. He mentioned, half-joking, that he could just use Priya's already-submitted forms "as reference" and fill them in rough.

Priya said: "Nahi bhai — kal wapas chalte hain. Mujhe koi dikkat nahi." (No, brother — let's go back tomorrow. It's no trouble for me.) They went back the next day. Both forms were genuine. Both were back-check verified at 100% accuracy.

📌 Value in Action: Integrity is sometimes something your colleague gives you — by not letting you cross a line in a moment of exhaustion. Priya didn't do this because she was afraid of the QA system. She did it because she cared about the work, and about Kamlesh, too much to let him compromise himself.
🗣️ Scenario · Accountability — Madhya Pradesh · The Two Wings

The Situation: In a 28-surveyor study in MP, Wing A's Supervisor Mangesh ran his team openly — surveyors could report non-contacts without fear, problems were escalated within hours, and data quality was consistently strong (back-check discrepancy under 3%). Wing B's Supervisor Dharmesh was technically capable but managed through fear — surveyors were publicly shamed for missed targets and began fabricating rather than report honest non-contacts.

In Week 5, GPS showed 22 forms from a village that had been "inaccessible due to flooding" for 4 days. Investigation confirmed systematic fabrication across 11 of Dharmesh's 14 surveyors. 67 forms invalidated. ₹1.4 lakh in remediation costs.

📌 Value in Action: Culture is set by the person at the top of the hierarchy that team members interact with daily. A Supervisor who creates psychological safety — where honest reporting is welcomed, not punished — produces better data than any monitoring system ever could. Accountability means saying what you know, when you know it. Not after the back-check catches it.

Policy 1 · Mandatory

POSH — Prevention of Sexual Harassment

J-PAL South Asia operates under the Sexual Harassment of Women at Workplace Act (POSH), 2013, and a broader Dignity at Work Policy covering all genders. Every field staff member — regardless of role, gender, or seniority — is covered and bound by this policy.

The Legal Test: "Unwelcome"

The test is NOT whether the conduct was intended as harassment. The test is whether the conduct was experienced as unwelcome by the person who received it. "I was just joking" is not a defence under POSH law or J-PAL SA policy.

How to Report — 6 Steps

1
You experience or witness harassment

You are NOT required to speak to the harasser directly. You may, if you feel safe, but it is never required.

2
Tell your Field Supervisor

Unless the Supervisor is the subject — in which case, skip directly to Step 3. Within 24 hours is recommended; there is no strict deadline.

3
Tell the Survey Coordinator (SC)

SC is legally required to transmit the complaint to the Internal Committee (IC) within 7 days.

4
If SC is involved: go directly to RA or IC

Every person has direct access to the IC at all times — you do NOT need to go through the hierarchy.

5
Anonymous reporting

You may report without identifying yourself. Anonymous complaints are investigated to the extent possible.

6
Request interim protection

You may ask for a route change, reassignment, or different accommodation while investigation is ongoing. SC must accommodate reasonable requests.

Zero Retaliation

Retaliation is any negative action taken because you reported — worse assignments, exclusion from team activities, unfair performance assessment. Retaliation is itself a POSH violation and treated with equal seriousness. If you experience retaliation: go directly to the IC. Direct IC contact: resops@jpal.org (Subject: POSH – Confidential)

3.1 · Why Research Ethics Is Regulated

This Is Not Bureaucracy. It Is Protection.

When you collect information from a person — about their household, income, health, or opinions — you are conducting human subjects research. This type of research has a history of misuse: experiments conducted on people without their knowledge, studies where participants were deceived, data used to harm the communities it was collected from.

To prevent this, research involving human participants now requires ethical review by an Institutional Review Board (IRB) before it begins. The IRB reviews the study design, the consent process, and the data protection plan to ensure the research does not harm the people it involves. Every J-PAL South Asia study has IRB approval.

What IRB Approval Means for You

The consent process you deliver and the data you protect are not optional extras — they are the conditions under which the IRB approved this study. Deviating from these conditions (skipping consent, sharing respondent data informally, interviewing an ineligible person) is not just a quality failure. It is a breach of the ethical and legal agreement under which the study was permitted to happen.

3.3 · Informed Consent in Practice

Consent Is a Process — Not a Signature on a Form

The most important sentence in research ethics: consent is valid only when four conditions are met simultaneously. If any one is missing, the consent is not legally or ethically valid — and the data collected under it is ethically compromised.

1
Information
The respondent must be told: who is doing the study, what it is for, what the data will be used for, that participation is voluntary, and what their rights are.
Invalidated by: "Ek short survey hai — bas hanji bolein" without any explanation of purpose or voluntary nature.
2
Comprehension
The respondent must actually understand what they have been told — not just hear it.
Invalidated by: reading the consent script at high speed while the respondent is distracted or doing something else.
3
Voluntariness
The choice to participate must be completely free — no coercion, pressure, or deceptive incentives.
Invalidated by: implying that government benefits, scheme access, or community approval depends on participating.
4
Capacity
The respondent must be capable of making an informed decision — not intoxicated, cognitively impaired, or in extreme distress.
Invalidated by: conducting an interview with a respondent who is visibly drunk, severely ill, or in acute emotional crisis.

🚩 Consent Invalidators — Know These

  • Coercion: "The sarpanch said everyone must participate" — consent must be voluntary.
  • Rushing: Reading the script in 30 seconds while the respondent is distracted — a signed form is not the same as valid consent.
  • Child signing: Children under 18 cannot provide consent for themselves — a parent or guardian must sign.
  • Proxy consent: "Her husband said it's fine" — the husband's permission is not the woman's consent. Each eligible respondent consents for themselves.
  • Non-literate respondents: When a respondent cannot read, the surveyor must read the entire consent form aloud before the thumbprint is obtained. "Signed" and "consented" are not the same.
🗣️ Field Scenario — Varanasi, Uttar Pradesh

Surveyor Manohar arrived at a household where Radha Devi (56) was grinding grain. Busy. He said "Ek chota sa form hai — bas hanji bolein" (It's a small form — just say yes), received a distracted nod, and began the interview. In the back-check: "Kisi ne aaya tha survey ke liye — mujhe kuch yaad nahi kya tha." (Someone came for a survey — I don't remember what it was about.) 📌 A thumbprint without understanding is not consent. A nod while distracted is not consent. The respondent must actually understand — and if they can't recall what the study was about in the back-check, you did not obtain valid consent.

5.3 · Detection System

The 5 Layers That Catch Fabrication — Why "Not Getting Caught" Is a Myth

Every surveyor who has been caught fabricating believed they would not be caught. This belief is false. The five layers of the detection architecture operate simultaneously — and they leave no reliable escape route.

L1
Duration Audit — Timestamp Analysis
SurveyCTO records exact time from first question to SUBMIT. A 35-minute survey completed in 8 minutes — consistently, across multiple days — cannot be explained as a "fast respondent."
Detection reliability: Very High
L2
GPS Verification — Location Cross-Check
GPS coordinates are cross-checked against the sample village coordinates. If your surveys show Village X but GPS shows you were at the town, the field office, or your accommodation — that is flagged immediately.
Detection reliability: High
L3
Back-Check — Re-Interview
10–15% of completed surveys are re-interviewed by an independent monitor. Key questions are asked again. Material discrepancies — or respondents who say "nobody visited us" — trigger an investigation of all forms from that surveyor.
Detection reliability: High
L4
Distribution Analysis — Statistical Pattern Detection
Surveyors who fabricate produce abnormally similar values across households — "clustered" data that doesn't match real population variance. Statistical analysis flags this pattern reliably.
Detection reliability: Very High
L5
Accompaniment & Spot Check — Live Observation
Supervisors and monitors observe live interviews (accompaniments) and verify that recently completed surveys actually happened (spot checks). Physical visits to households confirm that the respondent exists and can recall being interviewed.
Detection reliability: Definitive for checked surveys

5.4 · Data Confidentiality & PII

PII — What It Is, and the Rules That Apply to It

PII (Personally Identifiable Information) is any information that can be used to identify a specific person: their name, phone number, home address, Aadhaar number, health status, or financial details. When you collect PII in the field, you are handling information that belongs to the respondent — not to you, not to the project, and not to your WhatsApp groups.

✅ Do

  • Hand in all consent forms and listing forms to your Supervisor at the evening debrief — same day, every day
  • Keep PII documents in the labelled project folder/envelope during the field day — not loose in your bag
  • Use respondent codes (not names) when discussing survey progress with your Supervisor
  • Delete respondent phone numbers from your personal phone at the end of the project — in the supervised deletion session
  • Store project-related documents only on the project tablet — not personal phones or personal cloud storage
  • Raise concerns about PII security (documents getting wet, storage that isn't locked) to your Supervisor same day

🚩 Don't

  • Never share respondent names, phone numbers, or addresses in team WhatsApp groups
  • Never photograph consent forms, listing forms, or ID documents with your personal phone
  • Never keep consent forms in your personal bag overnight — give them to your Supervisor at debrief
  • Never discuss a specific respondent's personal situation, income, or health with other surveyors
  • Never save respondent phone numbers in your contacts under their name
  • Never share tracking sheets or survey data on personal email or personal drives
  • Never take project documents home

🚩 The WhatsApp PII Trap

The most common PII violation in field operations is a supervisor or surveyor sharing a tracking sheet screenshot in the team WhatsApp group — a sheet that includes respondent names, phone numbers, or health status. The sheet was meant for coordination. But WhatsApp is not a secure platform. If a phone is lost, stolen, or hacked — that PII is exposed. The rule is simple: tracking sheets, consent forms, and respondent details never go on WhatsApp.

The cost of raising it on Week 2, Day 1: a 30-minute investigation. The cost of raising it in Week 4: 83 surveys, ₹2.2 lakh, and a career setback for three people.

Q1 of 5
A Supervisor calls out a Surveyor's low count in the morning briefing in front of the full team: "Pankaj — sirf 4? Kya problem hai?" This is:
A. Acceptable — transparency about performance keeps the team motivated
B. Acceptable if done calmly and without personal insults
C. A Code of Conduct and professionalism failure — feedback is always private. Public accountability mechanisms feel like humiliation to the person at the bottom and create fear, not motivation.
D. Acceptable only if Pankaj was warned privately first
✅ C is correct. Feedback is always private. The Barmer scenario in Session 1 showed what happens when public accountability mechanisms replace private constructive feedback: the person stops sharing problems, routes around the supervisor, and eventually the team culture collapses. "Chaliye ek taraf baat karte hain" — always.
Q2 of 5
You notice that colleague Rajan's GPS coordinates across five surveys all show the same location, even though the surveys are from different households. You should:
A. Say nothing — it's not your responsibility to monitor a colleague's GPS
B. Raise it with your Supervisor using the escalation language: "Ek concerned observation hai — Rajan ke GPS coordinates 5 surveys mein identical lag rahe hain. Aap better judge kar sakte hain."
C. Ask Rajan directly why the GPS shows the same location
D. Wait to see if the pattern continues before raising it
✅ B is correct. Escalate to the Supervisor — not to Rajan. Confronting the person you suspect of fabrication can cause evidence to disappear and puts you in a difficult situation. Use the escalation language: specific observation, no accusation, "you can judge better." Same day. The Supervisor then investigates.
Q3 of 5
A Surveyor discovers on Day 14 that she recorded the wrong answer code for Q9 in five surveys from Week 1. What is the professional response?
A. Say nothing — the back-check probably won't catch a single question error
B. Wait until the back-check raises it and then explain
C. Try to access the submitted forms and correct them before the SC notices
D. Tell the Supervisor the same day, specifying which surveys and which question. Self-reported errors are treated as honest mistakes and trigger corrective action — not punishment.
✅ D is correct. Self-reported errors are treated fundamentally differently from errors discovered through QC. Reporting it is an act of professional integrity — and it protects you. An error reported by you triggers correction. The same error discovered by the back-check after you stayed silent triggers an investigation into whether it was deliberate.
Q4 of 5
A Supervisor asks a Surveyor to substitute a difficult-to-reach household with a nearby one that "seems similar" — without getting SC approval. The Surveyor should:
A. Tell the Supervisor: "Mujhe lagta hai humein SC se pehle poochna chahiye — kya aap unhe call karte hain?" and refuse the substitution until SC approves
B. Comply — the Supervisor is responsible for the decision, not the Surveyor
C. Comply but make a note in the form's comments field
D. Go directly to the SC over the Supervisor's head without telling the Supervisor first
✅ A is correct. Complying with a protocol violation does not protect you — it makes you a co-owner of the violation. The right approach: push back professionally using the escalation language, cite the need for SC approval, and ask the Supervisor to make the call to the SC. If the Supervisor refuses to involve the SC, you escalate to the PA directly. Option D (bypassing the Supervisor) should only be used if they actively refuse to escalate.
Q5 of 5
Which of the following best describes a professional approach to raising a disagreement with a Supervisor?
A. Raise it in the morning briefing so the whole team can weigh in
B. Mention it to other surveyors to build a consensus before raising it
C. Raise it privately, citing the specific instruction or protocol that creates the discrepancy, and ask for a check with the SC if needed — without attacking the Supervisor personally
D. Follow the instruction even if wrong, and flag it in a formal complaint later
✅ C is correct. Professional disagreement is: private, specific, referenced to evidence (a protocol, a training instruction, a policy), and focused on the issue — not the person. "Aapne galat instruction diya" (You gave a wrong instruction) is an attack. "Training mein X ke baare mein kuch aur bola gaya tha — kya hum SC se confirm karein?" (Training said something different about X — can we confirm with SC?) is a professional escalation.
