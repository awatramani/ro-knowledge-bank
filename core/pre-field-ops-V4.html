Demystifying the Field

Recently joined PA to RA:

"Why do we spend so much time planning data collection? It feels like the most intense part."

RA replies back:

"That is exactly the point. Planning is one of the most demanding phases, and often the most under-documented. This page is a practical roadmap to help us translate the research design into a workable field plan. It anchors the key discussions we need to have: what must be prepared, who needs to be involved, what 'good planning' looks like, and what support systems we can rely on when the field throws surprises at us."

The Core Philosophy

The Voice in your Head: "It's just a survey. We'll hire people, print forms, and go."

The Reality: Data collection is a logistics operation disguised as research. If you don't plan the process (permissions, payments, protocols), the science will fail. This document is your shield against chaos.

The Curveball & The Calm

The Curveball "You discover that the car vendor has declined to work just the night before the launch. The timeline is set, people are ready, but the cabs won't be there in the morning."

From chaos to controlled excellence: Fieldwork does not unfold on a tidy timeline. One week it is chaos to get everyone in the field, next week you come across a local festival closing half your sample. This is not a failure of planning; it is the context in which good planning proves its worth.

The Field Reality "A Surveyor approaches you looking flustered: 'Madam, the respondent started crying during the income module. I didn't know how to handle it, so I stopped.'"

The goal is not perfection: It is identifying the blockage and untying the knot. When a bus breaks down or a community leader pauses sampling, these are not disasters, they are routine roadblocks. Calm comes from robust planning, adequate training, clear protocols, delegation and trust in the people to deliver.

Mastering the Field Realities

It is tempting to imagine a clean start at 8:00 AM and smooth handoffs. Experience tells a different story. Treat every disruption — whether a breakdown or a refusal — as a routine hiccup to be solved, not a disaster to be feared.

How to build resilience:

  • Map the Grind: Do not announce a start/end time until you know the local context. If the only bus leaves at 4:00 PM, your plan must adapt to that reality, not fight it.
  • Collaborative Solutioning: When deviations hit, the RA and the Survey Coordinator need to collaborate to identify and apply solutions.
  • Trust & Delegation: Avoid micromanagement. Give field staff the freedom to apply their wisdom. Trust is the buffer that absorbs surprises.

Translation & Ethics

The Field Dilemma "The translation vendor says they need 5 more days. Your PA speaks the language fluently. Do you get your PA to translate it to save the deadline, or stick to the protocol?"

Before digitization, ensure your instrument is culturally accurate. A perfectly coded survey is useless if respondents do not understand the localized phrasing.

Do

  • Use certified linguists with NDAs.
  • Back-translate independently.

Don't

  • Use Google Translate.
  • Use internal staff for primary translations (risk of bias).

Hiring: Grit over Resume

The Hiring Conflict "Candidate A has 5 years of experience but interrupts you during the mock interview. Candidate B is a fresher who listens intently but fumbles with the tablet. Who do you hire?"

Hire for attitude, train for skill. Fieldwork is all about resilience. An experienced surveyor with a rigid mindset will corrupt your data quality much faster than a willing fresher.

The Test: Don't just ask survey questions. Roleplay a "hostile respondent" scenario during screening. See who stays calm under pressure.

For detailed information on hiring field staff, please click this link.

Piloting: Break Your Survey

The False Positive "You piloted in a quiet school nearby. It went perfectly. Day 1 in the field: The village is noisy, onlookers crowd the surveyor, and the respondent gets distracted. Your pilot failed."

A successful pilot is one that finds errors. If you come back saying "it works perfectly," you didn't test it hard enough. Test the commute, the environment, and the chaos, not just the tablet.

The "Stress Test" Rule

Don't pilot with friendly neighbors. Pilot with the busiest, most distracted respondent you can find in a chaotic setting. That is your real baseline.

Safety & Data Hygiene

The Judgment Call "It is 7 PM. A team is stuck with no transport. They want to walk 3km to the main road in the dark to save time. Do you approve?"

Data is never worth a safety risk. Field staff often take dangerous risks to "please" the RA or hit targets. You must explicitly authorize them to stop work and abandon the target if they feel unsafe.

Data Quality (HFCs) — Catch it early:

  • Duration: Too short = skipping; Too long = respondent fatigue.
  • GPS Variance: Are they at the household, or under a tree?
  • "Don't Know" rates: Lazy surveyors select this to avoid typing.
Pre-Field Ops › Planning and Preparing for Survey Operations

Why Planning Matters

The seamless appearance of field operations — where a surveyor arrives at the correct dwelling, fully equipped with a charged tablet, communicating fluently in the local tongue — is the direct consequence of relentless behind-the-scenes preparation.

This guidance assumes you are organizing substantial in-person fieldwork (e.g., 6,000 households across multiple states). With deployment mere weeks away for a team of 150 individuals, the margin between success and total disarray is established entirely during this preliminary phase.

Preparation is not merely charting timelines; it involves converting theoretical research designs into pragmatic field systems. Attempting to rush this timeline inevitably leads to poorly trained staff, missing resources, flawed sampling frames, and chaotic early days in the field.
The Roadmap

Planning Timeline: What Needs to Happen

Planning is a cascading sequence of milestones. Field training cannot commence until the digital form is tested; accommodations cannot be secured until cluster mapping is locked.

Pre-Field Ops › Mapping Households

Mapping vs. Census: What Is the Difference?

These two terms are often used interchangeably in conversation, but they are distinct activities with very different purposes. Understanding this distinction is fundamental before entering the field.

Physical Mapping

(Household Listing)
PurposeCreate a complete spatial register of every dwelling in the village so that no household is missed.
What you produceA numbered settlement map showing every dwelling, lane, and landmark, plus a total dwelling count.
Who & How LongA dedicated pair of surveyors physically traverse the settlement (0.5–2 days per site).
The AnalogyDrawing the blueprint of a building before you inspect every room.

Census Enumeration

(Building the Frame)
PurposeCollect basic demographic and screening data from every listed household to build the sampling frame.
What you produceA dataset with one record per household: head of household name, size, and screening responses.
Who & How LongIndividual surveyors assigned to zones on the completed map (1–6 days depending on count).
The AnalogyInspecting every room in the building using the blueprint as your guide.
The Key Insight & What Goes Wrong

Mapping ensures you do not miss anyone. Census ensures you know enough about everyone to select the right sample. Skip or rush either one, and the entire research project is built on a flawed foundation.

Field experience reveals a consistent set of deviations. Surveyors, under time pressure or lack of experience, skip peripheral settlements, secluded hamlets, or construction-worker shanties behind a market. Multi-household structures or chawls remain undercounted if surveyors don't enter floor-by-floor.

The Operating Model

As a Research Associate/Survey Coordinator, you are the architect and accountable owner of the listing operation.

As Planner

You sequence villages into batches, size teams to match settlement complexity, secure advance approvals, and build realistic timelines.

As Field Commander

You ensure the mapping method is followed precisely, that supervisors verify coverage in real time, and that exceptions are logged and escalated the same day.

As Quality Steward

You reconcile daily counts against map estimates, investigate discrepancies, and certify the final listing package before it is handed over for sampling.

Four Governing Principles

1. Exhaustiveness over speed

An extra half-day on a village is always cheaper than a revisit to fix gaps.

2. Method over memory

Surveyors who say "I know this area" are the ones most likely to miss a lane. Follow the method without exception.

3. Document over assume

Every locked household, boundary dispute, and ambiguous structure goes in the exception register.

4. Control daily, not weekly

The evening reconciliation is the single most important quality mechanism in the entire operation.

Pre-Field Intelligence & Planning

This phase happens 10–14 days before field deployment. The goal is to eliminate surprises. Every hour invested here saves three hours of crisis management in the field.

Why Pre-Field Maps Matter Any rule-based traversal method works well when dwellings are on roads or lanes. But in Indian villages, many households sit on open land, inside farms, behind orchards, or along footpaths (pagdandis). These households are systematically missed by road-based traversal. The pre-field intelligence exercise identifies them before the team arrives.

For each village, the PA opens satellite imagery (Google Maps/Earth) and systematically examines five dimensions: Household spread pattern, Road and lane networks, Peripheral habitations, Off-road households, and Natural/administrative boundaries.

Permissions and Community Entry

Community buy-in helps in carrying out field activities smoothly. 3–5 days before the mapping team arrives, the Supervisor visits the village to meet leaders (Sarpanch, Mukhiya, ward members, or respected elders).

After securing permission, the Supervisor asks leaders to help create a rough sketch map of the village, identifying boundaries, main habitations, peripheral settlements, and off-road households. This sketch is compared with the PA's satellite map to resolve discrepancies.

Settlement-Specific Protocols

Rural Settings

The Sarpanch's verbal endorsement is top-tier. If not possible, engage ward members. Follow up verbal endorsements with a WhatsApp message for documentation. Visit peripheral hamlet leaders separately.

Urban Slums

Identify leaders through Anganwadi workers or Municipal offices. Address suspicion directly: explain this is an NGO research project. Triangulate boundaries with 2-3 community members.

Apartment Complexes

Send a formal letter to the RWA President/Secretary 7–10 days prior. Follow up by phone after 3 days. Be prepared to present at the society meeting.

Sensitive Settings

Seek introduction through a trusted intermediary (health worker, teacher, religious leader). Deploy a smaller mapping team. Accept that multiple visits may be needed before mapping can begin.

The Traversal Method: Right-Hand Rule and Beyond

The right-hand rule is a deterministic traversal algorithm adapted from the "wall follower" method used in maze-solving. It converts a two-dimensional spatial problem (covering an entire settlement) into a one-dimensional sequential problem (following a single path).

SituationActionRationale
Street turns rightFollow the turn; continue numbering on the rightMaintains right-hand continuity
Street turns leftFollow the turn; right-hand side remains the referenceDirection changes but the reference wall does not
Side lane appears on the rightEnter the side lane; traverse it completely; return to main streetBranches must be fully explored before continuing
Dead endU-turn; number the other side (now on your right) back to junctionThe U-turn converts the left side into the right side
Emerge onto a previously traversed streetDo not re-enter; U-turn and complete the current branchPrevents duplication
Return to starting pointMapping is complete; verify all lanes coveredThe loop closes naturally
Critical Discipline Complete every branch of every street before moving to the next main street. If, while traversing a side lane, you emerge onto another main street, do not turn onto it. U-turn and complete your current branch.

Where the Right-Hand Rule Falls Short

The right-hand rule is a powerful starting point, but it has well-documented limitations in Indian field conditions:

Households not on any road: Dwellings on open farmland, hilltops, or across streams are invisible to road-based traversal (often 10-20% of households).
Ambiguous road definitions: Is a gap between two walls a lane? Different surveyors make different judgements, compounding errors.
Density bias & Interior loops: The rule spends more time in dense cores and may skip sparse peripheries or households trapped inside closed ring-road loops.

The 4-Step Strategy-Led Mapping Approach

1

Execute the right-hand rule along the road network.

The mapping pair traverses the settlement using the standard right-hand rule, numbering every dwelling on every road, lane, and pagdandi. This covers the connected portion of the settlement.

2

Conduct the peripheral sweep.

Using the PA's annotated map and the Supervisor's ground-truth sketch, the pair visits every peripheral habitation separated from the main cluster. Each habitation is treated as a mini-mapping unit with its own starting point.

3

Execute the off-road household sweep.

Accompanied by a local guide identified during village entry, the pair visits every household flagged as sitting on open land or disconnected from roads. These are numbered sequentially, recording GPS locations.

4

Cross-verify against satellite imagery.

After physical mapping is complete, the Supervisor compares the final map against the Google Maps imagery. Any dwelling visible on the satellite map that does not appear on the field map triggers a targeted revisit the next morning.

Logistics are not an administrative afterthought; they dictate data quality. Poor logistics lead to exhausted enumerators, broken devices, and rushed interviews. This guide translates decades of Indian field research into practical systems — whether you are managing teams in a dense Mumbai slum, navigating flooded kaccha roads in Bihar, or establishing base camps in tribal blocks.

Pre-Survey Geographic Planning

Context & Purpose: Effective planning begins 2-4 weeks before deployment. Assigning teams without assessing the geography leads to blown budgets, wasted fuel, and missed daily targets.

Step-by-Step Geographic Mapping

  1. Acquire Boundaries: Map district, block, tehsil, and Gram Panchayat boundaries. Plot sample HHs on QGIS or Google Earth.
  2. Cluster the Sample: Group sample points based on realistic daily travel limits (e.g., maximum 40 km radius per team).
  3. Assess Terrain & Seasons: Identify paved state highways vs. village kaccha roads. During the monsoon or Rabi harvest season, travel times can double.
  4. Network Mapping: Identify mobile network dead zones to plan offline SurveyCTO caching and designated end-of-day sync points.

  2. Clear Financials: Define who pays for tolls, parking, and driver meals/night halts to avoid mid-survey disputes.
  3. Logbooks: Implement the Daily Vehicle Log. The Team Leader must sign the logbook at end of every day.

Daily Rhythm & Field Coordination

Context & Purpose: Without a strict daily rhythm, teams lose focus, data goes unsynced, and safety issues escalate unseen.

The Standard Operating Rhythm

  1. 7:00 AM - Briefing: TLs distribute daily targets. Verify tablets/power banks are >80%. Ensure all enumerators have ID cards and referral directories.
  2. 1:00 PM - Mid-Day Sync: TL collects progress updates. Identifies blockers and redistributes HH assignments via WhatsApp.
  3. 6:00 PM - Debrief: Mandatory return to base. Connect tablets to WiFi hotspot. Sync all SurveyCTO/ODK forms. Complete the structured Daily Progress Report.

Equipment & Inventory Control

Context & Purpose: A dead battery or a lost tablet halts production instantly. Strict chain-of-custody must be maintained.

Device Configuration Protocols

  1. Naming Convention: Label physically (e.g., TAB-001) and digitally in the device settings.
  2. Lockdown: Uninstall all non-essential apps. Set screen timeout to 5+ minutes. Enforce PIN locks.
  3. GPS Settings: Ensure Location is set to "High Accuracy" (GPS + WiFi + Bluetooth).
