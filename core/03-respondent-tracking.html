Respondent Tracking & Attrition Management

Protecting study validity by ensuring every listed household is found and followed up
📍 Tracking ModuleVersion 1.0 · 2026⏱ 18 min read
The Foundation

Why Respondent Tracking Exists

Panel attrition is one of the most serious threats to the validity of a study. A 10% loss in the treatment group but a 20% loss in the control group makes any comparison meaningless. Your job is to make sure no one disappears.

New Surveyor asks:

"The listing says this household is here. They are not. Can I just mark them absent?"

Senior RA replies:

"No. Absent today is not absent forever. Your job is to find them — not to accept the first obstacle. A listed respondent who cannot be found is a tracking problem, not a closure. Work the protocol."

🔑 Why Attrition Kills Studies

If attrition is systematically different between treatment and control groups, you can no longer compare outcomes. Even a 5% differential attrition rate can invalidate years of work. Every respondent you track and interview protects the science.

The Visit System

The Three-Attempt Protocol

A single absence does not close a case. Every listed respondent receives a minimum of three contact attempts across different times of day before a status is escalated.

🏠 Attempt 1

First visit, any time of day. If absent, ask neighbors or household members when the respondent is typically home. Log expected return time.

🌅 Attempt 2 — Different Time

Return at the time neighbors suggested. Morning vs. afternoon matters: farmers are in fields by 7am; women may be home midday; men may return at dusk.

📞 Attempt 3 — Phone First

Call ahead if phone contact exists. Time the visit. If unavailable again, initiate supervisor escalation and log a scheduled callback.

Field Scenario: The Migrant WorkerRamesh Kumar, HH-034, is listed as household head. You arrive three times and his wife tells you he moved to Surat for construction work eight months ago. She does not know his address but has a phone number. Protocol: Do not close the case. Record the phone number. Attempt phone tracking with supervisor approval. If interview is possible by phone under the study protocol, proceed. If not, escalate for a decision — never substitute.
⚠️ Tracking Violations
  • Substituting a different household member without supervisor approval
  • Marking "Hard Refusal" after a single attempt
  • Guessing a status rather than recording what you observed
  • Using incorrect GPS coordinates to fabricate a visit location
  • Closing a migrated respondent as untraceable without phone attempts

🔑 The Paper Sheet Is Your Insurance

If your tablet dies, is stolen, or loses connectivity, your paper tracking sheet allows work to continue without data loss. Keep it complete, legible, and protected from rain. Never transcribe "later" — write at the moment.

End of Day

Closing Out Your Tracking Day

Every tracking day must be formally closed. Data not synced is data at risk.

Common Mistakes
  • Syncing data only "when I remember" — not nightly
  • Writing "will do tomorrow" on callbacks without a specific time
  • Leaving GPS location OFF because it drains battery
  • Marking a migrated case closed without phone attempts
What Good Looks Like
  • Nightly sync before sleeping — non-negotiable
  • Every callback has a specific date and time in the system
  • GPS enabled for all SurveyCTO sessions, always
  • Every migrated case has at least one phone attempt logged
🔑 Closing Rule

If your supervisor cannot see your tracking sheet and immediately know the status of every household on your list, your recordkeeping is not good enough. Clarity is not optional — it is the job.
